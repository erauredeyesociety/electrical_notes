This comprehensive review covers the theoretical foundations, essential equations, and homework components for **Lectures 12 through 15** of CEC 315, focusing on the transition from periodic to aperiodic signal analysis using Fourier Transforms.

### **Lecture 12: Continuous-Time and Discrete-Time Fourier Transforms**

**High-Level Theory**
Lecture 12 bridges the gap between periodic signals (Fourier Series) and aperiodic signals (Fourier Transform). The core idea is to treat an aperiodic signal as a periodic signal with an infinite period ($T \to \infty$ or $N \to \infty$). As the period increases, the discrete harmonic frequencies become more densely packed until they form a continuous spectrum.

**Relevant Equations**
*   **CT Fourier Transform (CTFT) Pair:**
    *   *Analysis (Forward):* $X(j\omega) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(t) e^{-j\omega t} dt$.
    *   *Synthesis (Inverse):* $x(t) = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} X(j\omega) e^{j\omega t} d\omega$.
*   **Discrete-Time Fourier Transform (DTFT) Pair:**
    *   *Analysis (Forward):* $X(e^{j\omega}) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} x[n] e^{-j\omega n}$.
    *   *Synthesis (Inverse):* $x[n] = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_{2\pi} X(e^{j\omega}) e^{j\omega n} d\omega$.
*   **Key Identity for Exponentials:** $\int_{0}^{\infty} e^{-\alpha t} dt = \frac{1}{\alpha}$ for $Re(\alpha) > 0$.

**Homework Components**
Students are expected to compute transforms directly from these definitions for standard signals, such as one-sided decaying exponentials ($e^{-at}u(t)$) and rectangular pulses. A universal "sanity check" often used is verifying that $X(j0)$ (or $X(e^{j0})$) equals the total area or sum of the signal in the time domain.

### **Lecture 13: Fourier Transform Properties and LTI Systems**

**High-Level Theory**
Lecture 13 introduces properties that allow for the construction of complex transform pairs from simpler ones without re-evaluating integrals. The **Convolution Property** is the most significant: it replaces the difficult time-domain convolution integral/sum with simple frequency-domain multiplication.

**Relevant Equations**
*   **Convolution Property:** $y(t) = x(t) * h(t) \leftrightarrow Y(j\omega) = X(j\omega)H(j\omega)$.
*   **Time Shifting:** $x(t - t_0) \leftrightarrow e^{-j\omega t_0}X(j\omega)$.
*   **Differentiation (Time):** $\frac{dx(t)}{dt} \leftrightarrow j\omega X(j\omega)$.
*   **Multiplication (Time):** $x(t)y(t) \leftrightarrow \frac{1}{2\pi} [X(j\omega) * Y(j\omega)]$.
*   **Duality (CT):** $X(jt) \leftrightarrow 2\pi x(-\omega)$.

**Homework Components**
The homework requires applying these properties to find outputs of LTI systems. This involves the **"Analysis Pipeline"**: transforming the input and impulse response to the frequency domain, multiplying them to find $Y(j\omega)$, and using **Partial Fraction Expansion (PFE)** to find the inverse transform $y(t)$.

### **Lecture 14: Magnitude, Phase, Group Delay, and Filtering**

**High-Level Theory**
This lecture shifts focus from purely magnitude to the importance of **Phase**. Phase spectrum carries critical information about signal structure and timing; scrambling it can completely change a waveform's appearance. **Group Delay** quantifies the effective delay experienced by different frequency components.

**Relevant Equations**
*   **Polar Form:** $X(j\omega) = |X(j\omega)| e^{j\angle X(j\omega)}$.
*   **Log-Magnitude (dB):** $|H(j\omega)|_{dB} = 20 \log_{10}|H(j\omega)|$.
*   **Group Delay:** $\tau(\omega) = -\frac{d}{d\omega} \angle H(j\omega)$.
*   **First-Order Group Delay:** For $H(j\omega) = \frac{1}{1+j\omega/\omega_c}$, $\tau(\omega) = \frac{1/\omega_c}{1+(\omega/\omega_c)^2}$.

**Homework Components**
Tasks include computing magnitude and phase values across various frequencies to fill out performance tables. Students must also test for **linear phase**, which corresponds to a pure time delay ($\angle H(j\omega) = -\omega t_d$) and constant group delay.

### **Lecture 15: First/Second-Order Systems and Bode Plots**

**High-Level Theory**
Lecture 15 applies Fourier tools to standard physical models governed by first- and second-order differential (CT) or difference (DT) equations. **Bode Plots** provide asymptotic approximations to visualize the frequency response quickly.

**Relevant Equations**
*   **First-Order CT Standard Form:** $H(j\omega) = \frac{1}{1+j\omega/\omega_c}$, where $\omega_c$ is the -3 dB cutoff frequency.
*   **Second-Order CT Standard Form:** $H(j\omega) = \frac{\omega_n^2}{(j\omega)^2 + 2\zeta\omega_n(j\omega) + \omega_n^2}$.
    *   $\omega_n$: Natural Frequency.
    *   $\zeta$: Damping Ratio.
*   **Resonance Peak Magnitude:** $|H(j\omega_n)| = \frac{1}{2\zeta}$.

**Homework Components**
The homework focuses on classifying systems based on their damping ratio $\zeta$ (underdamped, critically damped, or overdamped) and identifying resonance peaks. Students are also required to derive transfer functions directly from system equations by replacing $d/dt$ with $j\omega$ (CT) or delays with $e^{-j\omega}$ (DT).
