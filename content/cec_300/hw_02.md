def DME(pulse1, pulse2, pulse3, pulse4):
    """
    Simulate DME pulse analysis
    
    Parameters:
    pulse1, pulse2: transmitted pulses from aircraft to DME station
    pulse3, pulse4: response pulses received by aircraft
    """
    
    # Calculate pulse pair spacings
    tx_spacing = pulse2 - pulse1
    rx_spacing = pulse4 - pulse3
    
    # Calculate time between first pulses (round trip time)
    round_trip_time = pulse3 - pulse1
    
    # Channel definitions
    X_SPACING = 12  # microseconds
    Y_SPACING = 30  # microseconds
    X_DELAY = 50    # microseconds
    Y_DELAY = 56    # microseconds
    CONVERSION_FACTOR = 12.36  # microseconds per nautical mile
    
    # Check for garbled signals (invalid pulse spacings)
    valid_x_spacing = (tx_spacing == X_SPACING and rx_spacing == X_SPACING)
    valid_y_spacing = (tx_spacing == Y_SPACING and rx_spacing == Y_SPACING)
    
    if not (valid_x_spacing or valid_y_spacing):
        print("Signal Garbled.")
        return
    
    # Check for FRUIT (mismatched channels)
    if (tx_spacing == X_SPACING and rx_spacing == Y_SPACING) or \
       (tx_spacing == Y_SPACING and rx_spacing == X_SPACING):
        print("FRUIT detected.")
        return
    
    # Determine channel
    if valid_x_spacing:
        channel = "X"
        ground_delay = X_DELAY
    else:  # valid_y_spacing
        channel = "Y"
        ground_delay = Y_DELAY
    
    # Check if received signals arrive in less than built-in delay
    if round_trip_time < ground_delay:
        print("Signal Garbled.")
        return
    
    # Calculate distance
    propagation_time = round_trip_time - ground_delay
    distance_nm = propagation_time / CONVERSION_FACTOR
    
    # Check for no reply condition (exceeding 200 nmi)
    if distance_nm > 200:
        print(f"Channel is {channel}, no reply.")
        return
    
    # Print result
    print(f"Channel is {channel}, distance is {distance_nm:.2f} nm.")

## Problem 4: GPS Essay

Here's a comprehensive essay on GPS positioning:

How GPS Determines Three-Dimensional Position

Global Positioning System (GPS) receivers determine their three-dimensional position through a process called trilateration, which uses precise timing signals from multiple satellites. The fundamental principle involves measuring the time it takes for signals to travel from satellites to the receiver, then converting these times into distances.

The process begins when a GPS receiver captures signals from at least four satellites. Each satellite continuously broadcasts its precise location and the exact time the signal was transmitted. The receiver compares the transmission time with its own internal clock to calculate the signal's travel time. Multiplying this time by the speed of light yields the distance to each satellite.

With signals from three satellites, the receiver could theoretically determine a two-dimensional position (latitude and longitude). However, because GPS receivers typically use less accurate clocks than the atomic clocks on satellites, a fourth satellite is necessary to correct for timing errors and calculate the third dimension (altitude). This creates a system of four equations that the receiver solves to determine its position in three-dimensional space.

The mathematics behind this involves spheres in space. Each satellite's distance measurement defines a sphere centered on that satellite. The receiver's position is at the intersection point of all these spheres. In practice, the receiver uses sophisticated algorithms to solve for x, y, z coordinates and the time offset simultaneously.

Sources of Error Affecting GPS Accuracy

Several factors can introduce errors into GPS position calculations, affecting overall accuracy:

1. Atmospheric EffectsThe ionosphere and troposphere delay GPS signals as they pass through these layers. The ionosphere, containing charged particles, causes variable delays depending on solar activity and time of day. The troposphere, the lowest layer of the atmosphere, introduces delays related to weather conditions, temperature, and humidity. These atmospheric effects can cause errors of 2-5 meters.

2. Satellite Clock ErrorsAlthough satellites carry precise atomic clocks, minor timing inaccuracies still occur. Even nanosecond errors can translate to position errors of about 30 centimeters. The ground control segment continuously monitors and corrects these clock errors.

3. Orbital ErrorsSatellite orbits are predictable but not perfect. Small variations in orbital position (ephemeris errors) affect the accuracy of the satellite location data broadcast to receivers. These errors typically range from 1-2 meters.

4. Multipath EffectsSignals can bounce off buildings, terrain, or other surfaces before reaching the receiver. These reflected signals travel longer paths than direct signals, causing the receiver to calculate incorrect distances. Urban environments with tall buildings are particularly prone to multipath errors.

5. Receiver NoiseInternal electronic noise in the GPS receiver can affect signal processing and timing measurements. Better quality receivers with advanced signal processing can minimize this error source.

6. Geometric Dilution of Precision (GDOP)The relative positions of satellites in the sky affect accuracy. When satellites are clustered together or in a straight line, the geometry is poor, leading to larger position uncertainties. Optimal satellite geometry occurs when satellites are well spread across the sky.

7. Selective Availability (Historical)Until May 2000, the U.S. government intentionally degraded civilian GPS signals, introducing errors of up to 100 meters. This feature was discontinued, significantly improving civilian GPS accuracy.

8. Satellite Health and ConfigurationTemporary satellite outages, maintenance periods, or satellites at the end of their operational life can affect the availability and quality of signals.

Modern GPS receivers use various techniques to mitigate these errors. Differential GPS (DGPS) uses fixed ground stations to broadcast correction signals. Wide Area Augmentation System (WAAS) provides corrections over large areas. Assisted GPS (A-GPS) uses network resources to improve performance in challenging environments. These augmentation systems can reduce errors to less than one meter in many applications.

Understanding these error sources is crucial for applications requiring high precision, such as aircraft landing systems, surveying, and autonomous vehicle navigation. As GPS technology continues to evolve, new techniques and additional satellite constellations (like Galileo and GLONASS) are further improving accuracy and reliability.

b. Fiber-Optic Gyroscopes and the Sagnac Effect

Fiber-optic gyroscopes (FOGs) measure angular rate using the Sagnac effect. A laser beam is split and sent in opposite directions through a coil of optical fiber. When the gyroscope rotates, the beam traveling in the direction of rotation takes slightly longer to complete the path than the beam traveling opposite to rotation. This creates a phase difference between the two beams when they recombine. By measuring this phase difference, the system can determine the angular rotation rate about the axis perpendicular to the coil. The Sagnac effect is purely relativistic, occurring because light travels at a constant speed regardless of the observer's motion.
