Course Description
Computer engineering aspects; embedded sensor and actuator 
controllers; high-performance computing systems for air traffic 
control and weather systems; critical design factors including 
technological, economic, social, and professional considerations; 
techniques and practices in database management, human-
computer interaction, and system networks.


--- Page 6 ---
Course Goals
Students will learn to critically analyze how aviation regulations 
influence system design and evaluate the effectiveness of various 
navigation and communication technologies. They will develop the 
ability to model representative aviation systems using tools such as 
simulations or hardware to translate technical concepts into clear, 
actionable designs that address real-world aerospace challenges.


--- Page 7 ---
Student Learning Outcomes
1. Describe the breadth of computing systems and practices used in 
aerospace and aviation, including their technological and 
engineering components.
2. Analyze the performance of aerospace/aviation computing systems.
3. Evaluate the historical perspectives on computing in aerospace and 
aviation and their impact on current aviation systems.
4. Engage in debates/discussions about critical computing topics such 
as digital avionics, flight control systems, and air traffic 
management.
5. Design fault-tolerant computing systems, flight management 
systems, or human-computer interfaces (e.g., Glass cockpits, HUDs) 
using principles learned from case studies.
6. Implement models of avionics systems in simulation or hardware.

Module 1
•
Establishes a foundation for where we are today.  
•
The history of computing for aviation and aerospace are intertwined.
•
Standards define the requirements for aircraft or spacecraft components, systems, and systems of 
systems.
•
System certification must be addressed for airworthiness or “spaceworthiness” following processes 
established by a governing agency.  In the United States, this is the Federal Aviation Administration.


--- Page 20 ---
Module 2
•
Sensors provide the necessary inputs to enable guidance, navigation, and control of aviation and 
aerospace systems (e.g. Inertial Navigation Systems (INS), gyroscopes, magnetometers, 
compasses, air pressure systems, etc.)
•
Guidance Navigation (GNC) and INS must work together to maintain a control loop with sensor 
inputs being translated to vehicle actuation.
•
Signal processing techniques like Kalman Filters provides a means of estimating vehicle state based 
upon multiple sensory inputs.


--- Page 21 ---
Module 3
•
Avionics architectures can be classified as:
•
Federated (each function is its own line replaceable unit)
•
Distributed (integrated network connecting processors working on shared computing tasks)
•
Integration Modular Avionics (multiple functions integrated into common hardware)
•
Databuses enable communication and transfer of data between avionic systems.
•
ARINC is one of several agencies that produce databus standards used in aviation.
•
Timing and latency of a databus architecture must be approximated and improved to meet 
performance requirements of the air or space vehicle.


--- Page 22 ---
Module 4
•
Flight control has evolved to eliminate the risk of mechanical failures, reduce size, weight, 
and power known as Fly-by-Wire
•
Fly-by-Wireless uses similar concepts with wireless communication
•
Control systems enable flight stability and control of the vehicle.
•
Spacecraft attitude control will be examined in this topic.
•
Case studies of flight control computing will be examined investigating the flight control 
systems of the Airbus A320, the F-16 fighter jet, and Falcon 9 rocket.


--- Page 23 ---
Module 5
•
Flight management systems are onboard computers that support flight operations and reduce pilot 
workload.
•
Management of flight plans
•
Lateral Navigation (LNAV) and Vertical Navigation (VNAV) Guidance
•
FMS integrates inputs from GPS, INS, and radio navigation sytems.
•
FMS interfaces with with
•
Cockpit interfaces – provide situational awareness and guidance to pilot
•
Autopilot – enables efficiency, safety, and precision of part of or entire flight.


--- Page 24 ---
Module 6
•
Computing in aviation and aerospace is not limited to vehicle-based systems.
•
Air Traffic Management requires significant infrastructure (primarily ground-based, but some space-
based systems)
•
Includes:
•
ERAM – enroute airspace management
•
STARS – standard terminal automation replacement system
•
SWIM – system wide information management (infrastructure and standards for 
information to be exchanged across FAA systems)
•
Automatic Dependent Broadcast – Surveillance – tracks aircraft through a network of ground-based 
terminals.
•
UAS Traffic Management and AAM/UAM Traffic Management strategies require significant 
interconnectivity including a SWIM-like network backbone and vehicle-to-vehicle communications.


--- Page 25 ---
Module 7
•
Module explores topics for space-based systems including:
•
Spacecraft Onboard Computing: architecture, hardware, and software.
•
Deep Space Navigation and Communications are substantially different that aviation-based 
strategies
•
No compass or GPS to guide the system
•
Latency of communications increases proportionally with distance
•
Launch vehicles require their own computing systems to automate launch functions, monitor 
vehicle health, maintain desired trajectory, etc.
•
Autonomous docking & rendezvous.
•
In space, automation often guides a space craft’s rendezvous with satellites, space 
stations, etc. and in some cases dock with those systems.
•
Automated return and landing on ground-based or sea-based platforms


--- Page 26 ---
Module 8 
This module addresses more systems engineering focused topics applicable.
•
Design Assurance Levels are a means of identifying the criticality of hardware or software systems 
under development.
•
Standards define the development practice, documentation, verification, and validation of onboard 
systems.
•
Robustness is achieved through fault-tolerant design and redundancy.
•
Case studies shall be explored for failure cases in aviation and aerospace.
•
Safety assessment techniques are necessary for ensuring risk is sufficiently mitigated.  
•
Common approaches include:
•
Fault Tree Analysis
•
Failure Modes and Effects Analysis
•
Functional Hazard Assessment
