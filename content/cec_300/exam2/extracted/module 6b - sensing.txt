Overview
• Airborne detection involves the use of sensors onboard the UAS to 
sufficiently detect and track an intruder.
• Cooperative and non-cooperative solutions exist
• Cooperative – detection is dependent upon the equipage of the intruder with some 
cooperative technology.
• e.g. Transponder, ADS-B, TCAS II, etc.
• Non-cooperative – detection relies solely upon the sensor and sensor processing’s 
capabilities to detect and track an intruder.
• Sensors can be active or passive:
• Active – energy is emitted to aid the sensor in detecting the intruder
• Passive – relies solely on external stimuli

Radar (Primary)
• Most versatile non-cooperative sensor
• Field of regard achieved through:
• Antenna array
• Actively scanning (mechanical or electronically steered antenna)
• Scanning approaches:
• Must frequently revisit frame of tracked target, but does not want to waste time there versus 
exploring rest of the field of reference
• Must be considered
• Must prioritize intruders given range.
• Outputs:
• Range, range rate, azimuth, and elevation
• Standards developed by RTCA, SC-228,number DO-386.

Laser Systems
• Example: LIDAR – or laser range finder – emits laser light, which is reflected off of 
any surface it encounters.  
• The time of flight between emission and receiving the reflection is used to 
measure distance.
• Parameters include:
• Scan rate
• Range 
• Resolution (angular distance between measurements)
• Can determine (dependent upon specifications):
• Distance to target
• Bearing to target
• Trajectory of target
• Can operate through certain atmospheric features such as fog, but typically not 
suitable for aviation applications due to eye safety of other pilots

EO/IR Performance and Comparison
Electro-Optical/Infrared Camera (EO/IR) – combine 
visible spectrum (EO) and thermal (IR) imaging enabling 
surveillance, targeting, and tracking.
• Key Features:
•
Electro-Optical (EO): Captures high-resolution images in 
the visible and near-infrared spectrum, ideal for 
recognizing objects and identifying targets during daylight 
or well-lit conditions.
•
Infrared (IR): Operates in the thermal spectrum, detecting 
heat signatures emitted by objects, enabling imaging in 
complete darkness or through obscurants like fog, smoke, 
or dust.
• Applications:
•
Military and Defense: Long-range surveillance, targeting, 
tracking, and reconnaissance.
•
Security: Monitoring critical infrastructure, border 
security, and perimeter defense.
•
Search and Rescue: Locating survivors in low-visibility 
conditions or open water.
•
Law Enforcement: Crowd monitoring, riot control, and 
emergency response.

Acoustic Sensors
• Utilize an array of microphones and 
processors to analyze the sounds 
within the airspace surrounding the 
aircraft
• Can be capable of detecting and 
tracking local air traffic 
• NASA Access 5 study indicated 
concern about high signal-to-noise 
ratio
• SARA Inc.’s Terrestrial Acoustic Sensor 
Array (TASA) – acoustic phased array
• Detects aircraft, classifies threat, and can 
provide evasive maneuvers to 
automated vehicles such as UAS or UAM.

Overview
• Ground-based systems utilize UAS tailored or existing ground 
infrastructure to support detection within detect-and-avoid.
• Communication is a consideration:
• Communication of data with the UAS
• Communication of data with the GCS
• Like airborne sensors, these sensors can be:
• Cooperative – based upon suitable equipage for ownship and/or intruder
• Non-cooperative – based solely upon the sensor’s capabilities to extract

Ground-based Radar
• Ground-based radar can support the detection requirements of UAS detect-and-
avoid
• Organic: 
• UAS integrated radar
•
Radar is part of the integrated UAS “system-of-systems”, i.e. it is dedicated to the UAS system configuration and 
integrated with the control station and/or command and control datalink.
• Non-Organic:
• ANSP radar (e.g. FAA ATC Radar)
•
Air traffic control from Air Navigation Service Providers (ANSP) such as the FAA’s Air Traffic Control (ATC) / Air 
traffic Management (ATM) systems
• 3rd Part Surveillance Services (e.g. flightradar24.com)
• Ground-based Radar types:
• Primary Radar (cooperative and non-cooperative)
• Secondary Radar (cooperative only)
• Passive Radar (non-cooperative and cooperative)
• Note: these types are not exclusive to ground-based usage

Visual Observers (spotters)
• Observers the UAS flight and notifies pilot of UAS position, 
terrain/vegetation collision threats, and any perceived right-of-way issues 
with local air traffic
• Pilot-in-command has legal authority over the flight safety
• They are responsible for ensuring well-clear of other air traffic and not the spotters.
• Types:
• Ground-based Visual Observers
• Located strategically along the mission area / flight path.
• Airborne-based Visual Observers
• Located onboard a chase aircraft
• Observers that assist in taxi, runway situational awareness, etc. are 
sometimes called “marshals”
• Required for Part 107

Summary
• Ground-based technologies can be cooperative, non-cooperative, or a 
mix of both.
• ATC radar facilities typically provide both cooperative (secondary radar, ADS-
B) and non-cooperative (primary radar)
• Cooperative sensors include secondary radar, TIS-B, and wide area 
multilateration techniques
• Non-cooperative sensors include primary radar, passive radar, EO/IR cameras, 
etc.
• Visual observers can also play the role of non-cooperative detection
