– Integration of heterogeneous systems increases risk.
– Continuous innovation in defenses is essential.

Threat Actors and Motivation
•
Terrorist Organizations: Aim to cause financial losses, 
create widespread fear, and disrupt critical infrastructure.
•
Cybercriminals: Seek to instill fear, intimidate, steal, and 
engage in various forms of criminal activities.

Impact of Aviation Cyber-Attacks
Safety Compromises: Unauthorized access or manipulation of critical systems can directly impact flight safety and 
potentially lead to catastrophic events.
Financial Losses: Attacks can cause significant economic damage through operational disruptions, theft of financial 
data, and damage to reputation.
Data Breaches: Sensitive passenger data and intellectual property can be compromised.
Operational Disruptions: Attacks can lead to flight delays, cancellations, and overall chaos in airport operations.
Reputational Damage: Cyber incidents can erode public trust and damage the reputation of airlines and airports.
Theft of Intellectual Property and Intelligence: State-sponsored actors target the aviation industry to acquire 
valuable information.

Vulnerabilities Exist Across Systems and Sub Systems.
Here are a few to consider.
•
Flight Systems:
–
Software-intensive systems like flight control (including autopilot), displays, navigation, communication, engine control, landing 
gear, and collision avoidance systems can contain vulnerabilities introduced through human error, lack of quality control, 
malicious code, supply chain of software components.
–
Potential for unauthorized access and tampering with flight control systems.
•
Communications:
–
Unencrypted communication protocols, such as ADS-B, are susceptible to spoofing and manipulation and do not maintain 
confidentiality.
–
Vulnerabilities in Controller Pilot Data Link Communications (CPDLC) could be exploited.
–
Lack of (strong) authentication on critical data flows like data link can be exploited.
–
Weak integrity control on communication data flows allows for potential undetected modifications.
•
Surveillance:
–
Spoofing of ADS-B radio signals can present false aircraft information.
–
Spoofing of radar data over interconnection networks can mislead air traffic controllers.
–
Signal jamming of surveillance systems like Primary Surveillance Radar (PSR) and Secondary Surveillance Radar (SSR) can disrupt 
air traffic monitoring.
•
Navigation:
–
GPS spoofing or jamming can provide erroneous navigation information to aircraft.
–
Vulnerabilities in navigation aids like VOR and ILS could be exploited (though the technical difficulty varies).

Unauthorized Access
Unauthorized access refers to gaining access to systems, networks, or data without 
permission. This can involve exploiting software vulnerabilities, using stolen credentials, 
or bypassing security measures.
•
Event: RwandAir Cyberattack
•
Type: Unauthorized access
•
Actors Involved: SilitNetwork (suspected)
•
Description: Hackers gained unauthorized access to RwandAir's systems, exploiting 
weaknesses in the airline's cyber defenses. Used a combination of social engineering, 
phishing, and software exploits.  Loss of confidentiality and integrity of passenger 
data.  Near loss of critical operational functions.
•
Future Mitigations: Implementing stronger access controls, regular security audits, 
and employee training on cybersecurity

Issue: Firmware Vulnerabilities & Code Injection
• Firmware governs low-level hardware behavior in self-
contained avionics
• Unprotected firmware may be exploited via code injection 
or buffer overflows
– Example: Attacks against flight computer firmware can compromise 
control logic Secure boot processes and digital firmware signing 
mitigate these risks
• Strict code reviews and testing are applied in critical 
avionics systems
• Regular firmware updates are integral to maintaining 
operational security

• Challenges:
– Insider actions can lead to unauthorized software modifications
– Compromised update channels risk widespread exploitation in connected 
systems
• Mitigations:
– Aviation firms enforce strict access control and audit trails for updates
– Digital signatures verify the authenticity of software updates
– Continuous monitoring of update channels is vital
– Training staff to recognize social engineering increases overall security

– Collaborative design practices improve software robustness in avionics
– Thorough threat modeling ensures compatibility and security

System Wide Information Management (SWIM)
SWIM provides a single point of access for near real-time, 
relevant, and reliable aeronautical, flight, weather, and 
surveillance information.
•
Infrastructure and Standards: SWIM delivers the necessary 
infrastructure, standards, and services to optimize the secure exchange 
of relevant data across the National Airspace System (NAS) and the 
aviation community.
•
Data Sharing: It facilitates the data sharing requirements for NextGen, 
enabling increased common situational awareness and improved NAS 
agility.
•
Single Access Point: SWIM offers a single point of access for aviation 
data, allowing producers to publish data once and users to access the 
information they need through a single connection.
•
Operational Excellence: As the digital data-sharing backbone of 
NextGen, SWIM supports operational excellence and innovation in 
aviation.

Mitigations for Physical Hardware Threats
•
Risks:
– Direct access to hardware (flight computers, wiring) poses serious 
risks
– Aviation systems often use tamper-resistant enclosures
– Side-channel attacks exploit power consumption or electromagnetic 
emissions
– Continuous monitoring of physical integrity is recommended
•
Mitigations
– Hardened Enclosures: Using robust materials like steel or specialized 
alloys to make it difficult to physically access the components.
– Security Screws/Locks: Employing screws that require special tools to 
remove or locks that prevent unauthorized access.
– Encapsulation/Sealing: Covering components with a sealant or 
coating that becomes evident if tampered with.
– Ultrasonic Welding: Joining enclosure parts in a way that is difficult to 
reverse.
