Resolving Abort Button Error
• Step 1: Spoof the Mode (The Hack).
◦ Action: The crew must manually overwrite the Major Mode Register to display "71" (Program 
71).
◦ Why: Routine R11 checks the physical abort button. However, the logic dictates that if an abort 
is already in progress (P71), checking the button is unnecessary. By spoofing P71, R11 
"terminate[s]" its check, effectively silencing the broken button,.
• Step 2: Force the Throttle (The Consequence).
◦ Action: The crew must manually throttle up the engine and set the ZOOMBIT flag.
◦ Why: Because the computer thinks it is in an abort (P71) rather than a landing (P63), it does 
not automatically throttle up the descent engine. The crew must manually advance the throttle, 
then set ZOOMBIT to tell the guidance equations to take over control.
• Step 3: Cleanup (The Safety).
◦ Action: Reset the LETABBIT flag/register.
◦ Why: Once the critical phase is over or the landing is secure, the system must be restored so 
that a legitimate abort can be triggered if necessary.

•
The Issue: While over 10 miles from the landing site, the radar prematurely switched to 
short-range mode, becoming "nearsighted" and unable to lock onto the lunar surface.
•
Immediate Field Fix: Following a suggestion from Mission Control, the crew pulled the 
radar’s circuit breaker and then powered the system back on.
•
Operational Result: This "power cycle" reset the system, allowing solid radar data to 
begin flowing into the computer for a successful touchdown.
•
Mission Rule Contingency: If the fix had failed, rules permitted a landing without radar if 
the primary (AGC) and backup (AGS) altitude readings had less than a 1,000-foot 
disagreement.
•
Permanent Post-Mission Fix: To prevent noise-induced switching, range selection circuits 
were modified so the radar could only change modes when physically in the correct 
position.
