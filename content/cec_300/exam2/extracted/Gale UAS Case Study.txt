Aspect
Details
(a) System Developer
Aerosonde Pty Ltd (now Textron Systems)
(b) Sponsor
NOAA (in conjunction with NASA for some deployments)
(c) Storm Flown
* Tropical Storm Ophelia (2005, flown before becoming a hurricane) * Tropical 
Storm Noel (2007)
(d) Date Flown
* Ophelia: 2005; * Noel: November 2, 2007
(e) Summary of Results
•
Tropical Storm Ophelia (2005): This was the first deployment of an Aerosonde 
into a tropical cyclone. It gathered data in the storm's boundary layer, at altitudes 
as low as 1,200 feet (366m), which is too dangerous for manned aircraft.
•
Tropical Storm Noel (2007): The Aerosonde successfully flew into the eye of the 
storm, encountering hurricane-force winds. It reached a low altitude of 300 feet 
(91 meters). The UAS encountered 80 knots (40 m/s) of wind during its flight.
•
General Results: The Aerosonde deployments aimed to gather data from the 
lower parts of hurricanes, documenting and analyzing mesoscale circulations and 
improving understanding of the high-wind boundary layer. The data included 
temperature, wind speed, and atmospheric pressure. This data helps in better 
predicting the development of tropical depressions and storms into hurricanes.

Background
• Challenges to Aerosonde Campaign:
• Airspace access
• Previous Aerosonde mission ended with loss of aircraft in order to collect further data in storm
• Difficult to obtain a Certificate of Authorization
• Opportunities in:
• Naval Warning Area off of Key West in Gulf of Mexico
• Barbados in non-FAA flight information region
• Long ingress/egress – costal launch and transition to storm presented challenges
• Greater likelihood of aircraft loss / damage due to high winds 
• Much of vehicle’s endurance lost in transition
• Aerosonde is supposed to be recoverable, which means operation at very low altitudes was 
discouraged
• Cost
• Cost of platform
• Insurance underwriting
• Operational costs – flown by a team from AAI out of Australia

Key Requirements
• Shall deploy from P-3 free-fall chute into hurricane and operate for 60 min
• Shall acquire data from the sensor board at a rate of 2Hz for:
•  Air pressure with an accuracy of +/- 0.5 mb or better
•  Air temperature with an accuracy of +- 0.5 C
•  Relative humidity with an accuracy of +/- 5%
•  GPS position with error < 10 meters RMS
•  3-axis GPS velocity with error of 0.5 m/sec RMS

Repair of Stress Factures Issues
Problems
Solutions
• Repeated insertion and removal of Gale 
from sabot tube resulted in some 
fatigue damage
• When collapsed, tensile and shear 
stresses applied to screws and 
mounting plates
• Redesigned mounting plates out of 
aluminum
• Latch points added to body to ensure 
screws secure fit of mounting screws
• Note: aircraft weight increased as a 
result
• Resulted in cancelation of 2011 clear air 
flight test and 1-year to accommodate 
re-design and new flight window

pulled away deploying the parachute.

• Deployment
• Sabot Tube Pulled Away
• Falls like a dart (ballistic)

• Automation takes over flight controls

Post-Flight System Upgrades
• New release mechanism
• Once sabot tube reaches terminal velocity 
under drogue chute, released.
• Gale tethered in tube by wire.  Wire heated 
to break away and release for flight
• Lower cost autopilot 
• Pixhawk Autopilot
• Significantly lower cost compared with 
Piccolo SL
• Airframe redesign
• Lighter airframe
• 3D printing using biodegradable polymers
• Waiting for future flight opportunities
