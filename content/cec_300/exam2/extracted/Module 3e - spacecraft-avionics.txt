SPA Architecture


--- Page 16 ---
•
Origin:
o
Initiated in 2013 by Airbus, TTTech, and additional partners.
•
Purpose:
o
Reduce cost and complexity of hardware/software system
o
Combat obsolescence in aviation electronic systems
•
Approach
o
Line Replaceable Units integrated into a commercial backplane such as CPCI-E.
•
Replace faulty hardware
•
Upgrade obsolete / discontinued hardware
o
Time-triggered Ethernet (TTE) serves as the network backbone (more on TTE later).
•
Tradeoffs
o
Maintainability – highly modular with LRU’s enabling fast replacement of faulty or 
obsolete hardware, which lowers maintenance and upgrade costs.
o
Interaction Complexity – while LRUs are simple, their integration is complex 
governed by the system’s software and TTE network.

Low-speed & Control Buses
• MIL-STD-1553B: 
• Legacy standard, command/response protocol, 
reliable, but slow and heavy.
• CAN Bus (Controller Area Network): Adapted 
from automotive. Differential signaling which 
handles noise mitigation, multi-master, 
lightweight.
• RS-485: Simple, differential, high throughput for 
short distances, but no built-in error handling 
protocol.
High-speed
• SpaceWire / SpaceFibre
• High-speed, point-to-point (switched), full-duplex, 
2-400Mbps
• TTEthernet (Time-Triggered Ethernet)
• Mixed-criticality ethernet network, built from IEEE 
802.3 Ethernet standard with synchronized data 
slots.

Controller Area Network (CAN) Bus


--- Page 22 ---
•
Full-duplex with flow control w/ SpaceFibre being the next-generation successor to 
SpaceWire.
•
Topology: 
o
Network, not bus
o
Switched Fabric / Point-to-Point 
•
Protocols:
o
“Worm hole” routing – packets forwarded to destination before the entire message is 
received.
•
Performance:
o
SpaceWire – 2-400 Mbps 
o
SpaceFibre – up to 6.25 Gbps, longer cable distances, maintains quality of service, and 
built in fault detection isolation and recovery (FDIR)
•
Applications
o
James Webb Space Telescope (SpaceWire)
o
Supports remote sensing where each orbit can produce terabytes of data
•
E.g. Synesthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) or hyperspectral imaging.

Rad-Hard Processors:
o
Pro: immunity to radiation
o
Con: expensive and significantly lower performance compared to modern systems
o
e.g. RAD750, LEON
•
Commercial off-the-shelf (COTS) Processors:
o
Pro: High performance, cheap, low power
o
Con: Susceptible to radiation.
o
e.g. ARM Cortex, Snapdragon
o
Real-world: Ingenuity Mars Helicopter – uses Snapdragon for computing power to 
enable autonomous flight controls without any radiation hardening.
•
Hybrid/Heterogeneous Computing: 
o
Combination of COTS with RAD-HARD reliability
o
e.g. NASA SpaceCube – fast FPGA with rad-tolerant supervisor
Onboard Computing


--- Page 28 ---
•
Software-Defined Systems: Decoupling 
hardware from software. Reconfigurable radios 
(SDR) and satellites.
•
FDIR (Fault Detection, Isolation, and 
Recovery): Hierarchical autonomy (Device -> 
Subsystem -> System -> Safe Mode). 
o Layered Approach:
• Device Level: A memory bit flips? EDAC 
corrects it.
• Subsystem Level: A star tracker freezes? The 
computer switches to the backup.
• System Level: The battery is dying? The 
satellite autonomously enters 'Safe Mode,' 
pointing solar panels at the sun and shutting 
down non-essentials.
•
Autonomy Levels: From simple survival 
(power/thermal) to autonomous scientific 
discovery (filtering data onboard).
Examples of reconfigurable test platforms:
•
ESA OPS-SAT: A "flying laboratory" that allows 
the upload of new software apps in orbit to test 
novel communication protocols.
•
SmartSat: Lockheed Martin's architecture 
allowing satellites to be reconfigured like 
smartphones.
