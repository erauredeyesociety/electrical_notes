What is ADS-B?
•
As an alternative to secondary surveillance radar, ADS-B broadcasts 
information about an aircraft’s state within the airspace or on an airport 
surface.
o
Position, Horizontal Velocity, Vertical Velocity, Altitude (barometric and 
geodetic), and status information.
•
Part of a collection of related tools to ensure interoperability between 
different aircraft types and their equipage.
•
Provides position and velocity update information at 1 Hz.
•
Two types in the United States:
o
1090 Extended Squitter – over 18,000 ft.  (uses Mode S transponder)
o
978 MHz Universal Access Transceiver (UAT)


--- Page 3 ---
What is ADS-B?  Continued…
•
Automatic – does not require an interrogation, automatically broadcast 
during a specific time window each second.
•
Dependent – enables cooperative sharing of aircraft information between 
airspace users 
•
Surveillance – used to identify aircraft within the airspace and the 
attributes about it (position, altitude, horizontal velocities, vertical 
velocities, etc.)
•
Broadcast – information is broadcast such that a UAT or 1090 ES receiver 
can receive the information

Benefits of ADS-B
•
Provides more information about the aircraft state and the estimated 
accuracy and integrity of the information shared.
•
Informs not only ATC (like primary radar) but also other aircraft that can 
receive ADS-B input (ADS-B IN)
•
Less expensive: 
•
ADS-B System at airport is at least an order of magnitude cheaper than (e.g. 
$400k to $4M.
•
Less maintenance
•
Lower cost set up
•
Leverage existing infrastructure such as NDB towers
•
Smaller “error tails” than radar.

ADS-B In vs. Out Transponders
ADS-B OUT
•
Messages are sent from the aircraft, but no ADS-B messages are not received.
•
Required for compliance so that ATC systems and suitably equipped aircraft can use the information for 
situational awareness and collision avoidance
ADS-B In
•
Transponder receives the ADS-B messages from local air traffic, processes them, and displays this 
information on a suitable display such as:
•
Glass cockpit display such as Garmin G1000 connected to a Garmin GDL 90 or GDL88 Transponder.
BOTH Require:
•
Antennas at the top and bottom of the aircraft for broadcast and receipt.
•
GPS hardware including antenna.


--- Page 9 ---
Other ADS-B Group Messages
ADS-R (Automatic Dependent Surveillance –Re-broadcast)
•
Enables aircraft on 1090ES to see UAT aircraft updates, and vice-versa.
•
Ground station receives each frequency and rebroadcasts messages to the other frequency.
TIS-B (Traffic Information System – Broadcast)
•
Broadcasts traffic information collected by ATC from non-ADS-B equipped aircraft
•
Includes ground-based traffic with the ASDE-X (Airport Surface Detection System – Model X)
FIS-B (Flight Information Services – Broadcast
•
Ground-based system information is broadcast to suitably equipped UAT aircraft
•
Includes aeronautical data, weather data, and information related to the status of the national airspace to 
keep pilots informed.

ADS-B Challenges
1- Spectrum congestion at 1090 MHz 
Hybrid system can help reducing congestion (ADS-B + TCAS).
2- GNSS not available
Have backup surveillance systems such as Mode S.
Airlines will be required to check GNSS coverage before taking off.
3- Privacy of operations
ADS-B standards for anonymous identifiers.
4- Malicious spoofing
Have backup surveillance systems such as Mode S.

Current Issue
• Lack of privacy – ADS-B messages are not encrypted
o This is good news for sites like FlightRadar24.
• Dependent upon status of GPS and its reception by aircraft
• 1 Hz is too slow
• 1090ES congestion (must deal with Mode A/C and Mode S transponders)
• Easy to spoof
• Expanding to more types of operations
