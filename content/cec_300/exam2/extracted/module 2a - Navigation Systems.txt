• A radar mile is the time required for a signal to travel out to a 
target 1 nautical mile away and return. 
• Time= 2 x distance/speed of light
• Radar Mile = 2 x 1852m/3x10^8 m/s = 12.3 microseconds

•
The DME ground station adds a fixed delay of 50 microseconds before retransmitting 
the pulse (for X channel).
•
Distance in nm = (time – 50 microseconds)/12.3 microseconds
•
The maximum range allowed for use in FARs is 200nm.
•
200nm*12.3microseconds+ 50 microseconds = 2510 microseconds
•
DME can be used to LOS which can be up to 300nm.

--- Page 34 ---
FRUIT
•
There can be a lot of aircraft interrogating a DME transponder.
•
Many of the pulses coming back to the aircraft are responses to DME pulses from 
other aircraft.
•
These replies are known as Friendly Replies Unsynchronized in Time. 
o
Commonly called FRUIT.
•
The circuit to remove these unwanted replies is a deFRUITer. 


--- Page 35 ---
Garble
•
A valid pulse pair for the X channel will have a pulse spacing of 12 us. 
•
Suppose a pulse from other aircraft pulses winds up in 12us window.
•
The pulse spacing is not valid for any channel and the DME will not respond to these 
pulses.
•
Interference that scrambles the signals and prevents a correct output is called 
GARBLE. 


--- Page 36 ---
•
If two aircraft’s pulses cause garble, the aircraft will retransmit the pulses.
o
The new pulses could garble
•
Repeat  …. Forever.
•
To avoid this problem the interrogations are issued with random delays.
•
The average rate is 15 pulse pairs/second
Random Interrogations

Glideslope:
The glideslope systems provides vertical angle information to the 
pilot, so a fixed descent rate can be achieved.
The principle of operation is very similar to one of the localizer.


--- Page 53 ---
Glideslope (contd.):
There is one glideslope transmitter per runway.
Frequency range: UHF 329 – 335 MHz, with 20 channels.
Vertical range: 1 km.
Typical inclination: 3⁰
Typical deviation: +- 0.7⁰ (1.4⁰ total).
