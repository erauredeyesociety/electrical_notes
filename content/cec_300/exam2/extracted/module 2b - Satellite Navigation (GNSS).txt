respect to a coordinate system.
o Consistently calculate positions relative to an 
origin following the same model of the Earth’s 
ellipsoid relative to the equator and prime 
meridian

o Shape of the Earth (irregular and modeled as an 
ellipsoid
o Center of Mass
o System to define lines of latitude and longitude on 
the ellipsoid

Global vs. Local Datums
Global
•
Covers entire Earth’s surface fixed to Earth’s 
center
•
Used by GNSS systems
•
Standards:
o WGS (World Geodetics System)
•
US Department of Defense
•
e.g., WGS84
o ITF (International Terrestrial Reference Frame)
•
International Earth Rotation and Reference Systems Service.
•
e.g. ITRF2024
•
Challenge: positions of features can change 
over time
•
Updated periodically
Local
•
Avoids the challenge of global
•
Covers a local area with a local feature 
identified as the zero reference.
•
Work well with uniform tectonic motion
Dynamic vs. Static Datums
o Static - remain generally fixed with few periodic 
changes
o Dynamic – updated more frequently due to the 
constant non-uniform tectonic changes.

Epoch
•
Moment in time
•
Serves as reference in time-varying measurements
•
e.g. wind measurements at a weather station needs to have a epoch for the time of 
measurement
•
Often shown as a decimal number
o
year.wheninyear, 
•
When day of year/360, i.e. the percentage of the year completed.
o
e.g. January 1, 2021, 0 days or 0% of year, epoch = 2021.00.
o
e.g. May 5, 1991, day 128 or 35% of year, epoch = 1991.35
•
“The combination of datum + epoch is called a reference frame.”

WGS84
•
Standard used when publishing/sharing waypoint 
coordinates for navigation worldwide (e.g. runways, navaids, 
intersections, etc.).
•
Provides: coordinate system, reference ellipsoid, and 
additional models
o
No deformation model
•
Over time, objects at referenced coordinates can move and 
their coordinates change over time.
o
e.g. an underwater seismic event causes the gap between 
two plates to grow.  If two buoys were anchored to their 
respective plates, their coordinates will shift based on the 
deviation of the surface.
•
Observations will now see them as further apart as 
they have moved relative to the coordinate system.
o
Therefore, WGS84 is an example of a dynamic model

Latitude
•
Latitude is comprised of parallels, which are equally spaced circles around the earth 
paralleling the equator.
•
Parallels are designated by their angle north or south of the equator (10°, 20°, etc.) .
•
The equator is 0° latitude, and the north and south poles are at 90° angles from the 
equator.
•
The linear distance between parallel (latitude) lines never changes, regardless of their 
position on the earth.

What is GNSS?
•
A GNSS or Global Navigation Satellite System is comprised of satellites. For example, 
with GPS, each satellite transmits two codes (C/A and P-Code) to ground receivers 
and information about is ephemeris (including location, orbital path, etc.).

Enables:
o
Navigational positioning (global position)
o
Timing solutions (atomic clock-like performance and accuracy synchronized with 
UTC clock)
•
Examples:
o
Navstar GPS System, GLONASS, etc.

Basic Concept
•
Consider a GPS receiver and GPS satellite produce a C/A code (OR P-code) at a given 
time.
o
Through autocorrelation, the phase shift distance between the two signals can be 
measured.
o
The pseudorange of the satellite to the receiver can be approximated based on the 
phase shift and almanac data indicating the location of the satellite on Earth.
•
P-code follows a similar approach, but given its size, the C/A code can help identify 
the approximate distance, which can aid the autocorrelation of the P-code.

--- Page 91 ---
Differential GPS
•
Differential GPS is an augmentation of traditional GPS.
•
A base station is established at a surveyed location.
o
Given its known position, the base station receiver identifies corrections to the 
received signals.
o
Many of the corrected errors can be attributed to factors such as ionospheric noise, 
which would have similar impact on other receivers within the vicinity
o
The corrections are shared with suitably equipped GPS receivers
•
Suitably equipped receivers will receive both the GPS signal and the corrections, 
which results in a significant reduction in error.
