### 11. DAA Sensor Architecture — BVLOS UAS near a hazy, glaring, rainy coastline

A robust DAA architecture for this environment combines three sensors operating on dissimilar detection physics: ADS-B IN as the cooperative-RF layer, a small air-to-air primary radar as the active-RF non-cooperative layer, and an EO/IR dual-band camera as the passive-optical layer. The three are selected so that each layer's blind spots fall inside another layer's strengths, and so that no single environmental or adversarial event blinds the entire stack at once.

ADS-B IN delivers 1 Hz position, velocity, altitude, and identity from cooperative traffic at ranges well beyond the horizon, and at roughly an order of magnitude lower equipment cost than radar. Its core weakness is that it can only see equipped, truthful broadcasters; unequipped drones, gliders, and birds are invisible to it, and a spoofed extended squitter is indistinguishable from a real one. The radar closes both gaps. As a non-cooperative sensor it derives range, range-rate, azimuth, and elevation from the reflected echo regardless of whether the intruder transmits anything, and an independent radar track can corroborate or discredit a suspicious ADS-B report before that report drives an avoidance maneuver.

Radar, however, has its own characteristic failures. Small, low-RCS targets blend into clutter, ground returns from a coastline complicate target discrimination, and antenna aperture is constrained by the SWaP budget of a small UAS. The EO/IR camera fills these gaps. The EO band offers high angular resolution for classification in clear daylight at ranges near 20 km, while the IR band sees through haze and persists at night through thermal contrast out to roughly 35 km. EO/IR is in turn defeated by direct sun glare in the visible band and by heavy rain in both bands, but in precisely those conditions the radar's RF return is unaffected by optical obscurants and ADS-B remains weather-agnostic.

The three sensors fail in different directions, and that offsetting of failure modes is the design's central virtue. Stacking cooperative RF broadcast, active RF echo, and passive optical means no single environmental failure (haze, glare, rain) and no single adversarial failure (spoofing, non-cooperative target) blinds the whole system simultaneously, which is precisely the Swiss-cheese principle applied to DAA.

### 12. Cybersecurity Scenario — False ADS-B targets near a busy airport

A spoofing attack that injects false ADS-B targets near a busy airport distorts decision-making at two distinct levels of the air traffic system, and each distortion compounds the other. At the ATC level, ghost tracks populate the controller display indistinguishably from legitimate aircraft. The controller is forced to either issue vectors around the phantoms — wasting airspace, sequencing time, and frequency capacity — or risk masking a real converging aircraft amid the injected chaff. Either response degrades situational awareness and raises workload during exactly the peak period the attack was timed to exploit. At the airborne level, TCAS and ACAS systems treat a spoofed extended squitter as genuine traffic, and a well-placed ghost can trigger an unnecessary Resolution Advisory that forces a climb or descent into airspace that was previously clear. Even when the spoof is eventually unmasked, the cumulative false-alarm rate erodes pilot trust in the automation and desensitizes crews to the next genuine RA.

A mitigation at the physical layer addresses the attack through sensor cross-validation. ATC and airborne systems correlate every ADS-B report against independent measurements: secondary radar range and azimuth, multilateration (time-difference-of-arrival on the 1090 MHz signal across three or more ground receivers), and, onboard, air-to-air primary radar. A spoofer would have to simultaneously fake a coherent RF echo and a geometrically consistent TDOA fingerprint, which is far more difficult than broadcasting a message. A second mitigation operates at the algorithmic and protocol layer through anomaly detection and authentication. Statistical monitors flag kinematically impossible tracks, duplicated ICAO addresses, reports that fall outside the published service volume, and sudden position jumps. The longer-term protocol fix is cryptographically signed squitters, allowing receivers to reject unsigned or wrongly signed messages outright.

These two mitigations fail independently, and that is what makes the combination strictly stronger than either alone. A spoofer who defeats the physical check by somehow synthesizing a consistent RF return must still defeat signed-message validation, while an attacker who forges signatures must still produce a physically plausible echo. Combined, the two layers drive attack success down multiplicatively rather than additively, applying the same layered-defense logic the course extends to DAA more broadly.

### 13. Spacecraft Control — Reaction wheels + magnetorquers on a small satellite

Reaction wheels produce fine pointing by exchanging angular momentum with the spacecraft body, but external disturbance torques such as solar-radiation pressure, atmospheric drag, and gravity gradient continuously pump momentum into the wheels. Each time a wheel spins up to counteract a disturbance, it stores slightly more angular momentum. Eventually the wheel reaches its maximum speed and saturates, at which point it can no longer absorb additional torque in that direction and pointing authority is lost in that axis.

Magnetorquers provide the off-loading path that prevents this saturation. A current-carrying coil inside the satellite generates a magnetic dipole **M**, and the interaction of that dipole with Earth's local magnetic field **B** produces a torque **T = M × B**. By driving **M** in the appropriate direction, the attitude-determination-and-control system can bleed stored momentum out of the reaction wheels over an orbit or two — a process called momentum dumping — without expending propellant. Three orthogonal coils give the system 3-axis capability. The technique works in low Earth orbit precisely because Earth's magnetic field is strong enough there to deliver useful torque.

The key operational limitation follows directly from the torque equation: because **T = M × B** is always perpendicular to **B**, no torque can be produced along the field direction at a given instant. The control system must therefore wait for orbital motion to rotate **B** through all axes, which makes desaturation slow compared to thrusters and ineffective in environments where no ambient field exists, such as interplanetary cruise or deep-space missions.

### 14. Surveillance-System Comparison — ADS-B vs radar around a BVLOS UAS

ADS-B and radar each excel in different operational regimes around a BVLOS UAS, and their respective strengths are nearly mirror images of each other. ADS-B is clearly the better choice for long-range, beyond-radar-horizon encounters at low altitudes over remote terrain. It delivers identity, GPS-precision position, and velocity at 1 Hz from any equipped aircraft within radio line of sight, long before a small air-to-air radar could detect that aircraft, and at roughly an order of magnitude lower equipment cost than the radar. Radar, in contrast, is clearly better in any encounter involving a non-cooperative intruder — an unequipped drone, a glider, an aircraft with a transponder failure, or a deliberately malicious target. Because radar derives range and bearing directly from the reflected echo, its detection capability does not depend on the intruder broadcasting anything.

Each system also exhibits a distinctive failure mode that the other does not share. ADS-B is uniquely vulnerable to spoofing, since its messages are unauthenticated and unencrypted; a commodity transmitter can inject plausible ghost traffic that receivers have no intrinsic way to distinguish from real broadcasts. Upstream GPS jamming or spoofing further corrupts ADS-B position reports without the broadcaster ever realizing it. Radar's distinctive failures, by contrast, are physical rather than informational. Line-of-sight masking by terrain hides targets below the horizon or behind obstructions, rain and ground clutter degrade certain frequency bands, and SWaP constraints on a small UAS limit antenna aperture, declared range (typically 3–8 nautical miles), and discrimination of small targets such as birds.

Neither system alone is sufficient for robust DAA, because each one's blind spots are exactly the regime where the other excels. The course's Swiss-cheese principle calls for complementary sensors so that one sensor's detection holes are covered by another's. ADS-B's blind spots — non-cooperative traffic and spoofed reports — align with radar's strengths, while radar's blind spots — beyond-line-of-sight detection and SWaP-limited range — align with ADS-B's strengths. A layered ADS-B + radar architecture therefore represents the minimum viable DAA sensor suite for BVLOS operation.

### 15. AI/ML Current Event — Adversarial vulnerability of deep-learning ADS-B anomaly detectors

The selected example is a peer-reviewed paper by Luo, Wang, Tian, Liu, and Yang titled "Adversarial Attacks against Deep-Learning-Based Automatic Dependent Surveillance-Broadcast Unsupervised Anomaly Detection Models in the Context of Air Traffic Management," published in *Sensors* 24(11):3584 on 2 June 2024 (DOI 10.3390/s24113584). Because the source is a peer-reviewed journal article rather than a vendor press release, the reported numbers carry substantially more weight than marketing claims: the methodology, dataset, training procedure, and threat model are all disclosed, and the experiments use the publicly accessible OPENSKY ADS-B archive, which makes the central claims independently reproducible.

The application studied is unsupervised anomaly detection on streaming ADS-B trajectories, intended as an algorithmic backstop against the spoofing and message-injection attacks discussed in Question 12. The authors train four deep architectures — a variational autoencoder, an LSTM autoencoder, a GRU autoencoder, and an LSTM encoder-decoder — on 50 OPENSKY flights and evaluate on 40 held-out flights with simulated injected anomalies covering position deviation, velocity offset, denial-of-service, and altitude drift. The problem these detectors are meant to solve is exactly the protocol exposure framed in Question 5: ADS-B is unauthenticated, so an algorithmic monitor that flags kinematically implausible reports is one of the few defenses available without changing the standard.

The evidence the source actually provides is a clean comparison of clean-input performance against adversarial performance under a TNAI-FGSM attack at perturbation budget ε = 0.05. On clean inputs every detector clears 90% accuracy and 90% F1 — the variational autoencoder reaches 93.69% accuracy and 93.79% F1. Under TNAI-FGSM the same model collapses to 51.55% accuracy and 13.59% F1, with the false-positive rate jumping from 4.93% to 91.05%; the other three architectures degrade by similar magnitudes. The authors also test a concrete mitigation, reporting that adversarial training restores accuracy above 75% across all four architectures.

The most plausible machine-learning paradigm is unsupervised deep learning on time-series data, with the autoencoder-style reconstruction-error scoring described in the course AI/ML module. Adequate training data for a fielded deployment would have to extend well beyond 50 flights to thousands of trajectories spanning regions, aircraft types, weather conditions, and traffic densities, paired with continuously regenerated adversarial examples. The conclusion supported directly by the source is that current deep-learning ADS-B anomaly detectors are catastrophically vulnerable to gradient-based adversarial perturbations and that adversarial training is a partial remedy. The engineering inference going beyond the source is that any real fielded ADS-B anomaly detector should be paired with a non-ML cross-check such as secondary radar or multilateration, because the ML layer alone has demonstrably brittle failure modes.

The principal technical limitation is the same brittleness Question 6 frames at the dataset level: a model trained without adversarial examples generalizes poorly to adversarial inputs, and the F1 collapse from 93.79% to 13.59% is the quantitative manifestation of that failure. The principal safety and certification concern is that an ML-based monitor whose accuracy falls below 52% under attack is functionally a coin flip, which would not survive any reasonable certification basis for a safety-critical surveillance function. The result connects directly to two course modules at once — the AI/ML module's warning about adversarial robustness and the M7 Cybersecurity module's framing of ADS-B as an unauthenticated channel where algorithmic defenses are necessary but not sufficient.

### 16. Integrated System Design — Layered Collision Avoidance for Urban Air Mobility

A layered collision-avoidance architecture for a UAM aircraft operating near conventional traffic consists of four concentric layers. The first is cooperative surveillance, comprising ADS-B IN/OUT alongside a Mode S transponder with the 1090ES extended squitter. The second is non-cooperative sensing, built around a compact air-to-air primary radar as the principal sensor and backed by an EO/IR dual-band camera that contributes bearing refinement and target classification. The third is an ACAS Xu avoidance-logic core that fuses every available track. The fourth is a human-oversight interface that gives the remote pilot supervisory authority over the avoidance loop.

Information flows from the periphery of the system inward toward the avoidance logic. ADS-B IN supplies cooperative tracks with identity and GPS-precision state at 1 Hz, out to radio line of sight. The Mode S transponder broadcasts ownship state for ATC, for nearby TCAS and ACAS systems, and for air-to-air interrogation, and it can itself perform air-to-air Mode S as a redundant cooperative channel. The radar generates non-cooperative tracks containing range, range-rate, azimuth, and elevation, which the fusion engine correlates against the ADS-B picture; the EO/IR camera narrows bearing and classifies ambiguous returns where radar alone is uncertain. All tracks feed ACAS Xu, which uses its probabilistic dynamics model, its probabilistic sensor model, and an offline-optimized Markov-decision-process lookup table to compute both a Remain-Well-Clear guidance cue during routine encounters and, when an encounter escalates, a vertical or horizontal Resolution Advisory.

Human oversight is structured to match the urgency of the encounter. For routine Remain-Well-Clear events the remote pilot operates human-on-the-loop: the system displays the recommended maneuver and the pilot approves or amends it before execution. Inside the Collision-Avoidance threshold the timing budget collapses, so ACAS Xu switches to automated mode and begins executing the Resolution Advisory within the TA-to-RA tau budget while notifying the pilot concurrently, who retains override authority consistent with PIC responsibility.

The architecture is designed to remain effective under at least two plausible failure cases. The first is spoofed or lost ADS-B. ACAS Xu is configured so that a Resolution Advisory may be issued from ADS-B alone only when active surveillance — radar or air-to-air Mode S — corroborates the cooperative report; otherwise the logic falls back to active sensing entirely. A spoofer who injects a ghost track produces no radar echo and no Mode S reply, so the logic discards the track rather than maneuvering on it. If ADS-B is lost entirely through jamming, the radar, Mode S, and EO/IR stack continue to provide separation. The second failure case is GPS denial. Loss of ownship GPS degrades ADS-B outputs across the entire cooperative layer. The design mitigates this by navigating on INS and altimeter for short-term state, continuing to transmit Mode S without the ADS-B position validity flags, and relying on radar-derived relative geometry for avoidance. Remain-Well-Clear margins automatically widen in this degraded mode to reflect the increased uncertainty in the ownship state estimate.

The tradeoff this architecture intentionally accepts is the SWaP, cost, and integration burden of carrying a small radar on a UAM vehicle when ADS-B alone would be roughly an order of magnitude cheaper to equip and would be adequate in fully-equipped airspace. The reason is structural rather than economic. The attack surface and equipage gap the course explicitly warns about — non-cooperative intruders, ADS-B spoofing, and GPS denial — all fail in directions that ADS-B alone cannot recover from. Saving the radar cost buys a sensor suite whose remaining holes are all correlated, sitting downstream of a single RF broadcast chain, while paying the radar cost buys uncorrelated failure modes that preserve separation in the two most plausible UAM failure scenarios. The SWaP penalty is a predictable engineering cost that can be budgeted in advance, whereas the correlated-failure alternative cannot.
