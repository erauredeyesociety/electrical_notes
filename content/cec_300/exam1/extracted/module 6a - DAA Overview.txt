potential collision

Uncrewed – automated or remotely piloted avoidance
•
ACAS Xu

and non-cooperative collision avoidance through 
• Remain Well Clear  - changes to course to avoid a 
collision avoidance maneuver
• Collision avoidance – a maneuver to mitigate the risk 
of a mid-air collision

Notes on terminology (2)
• From RTCA DO-365:
• Collision Avoidance:
• “Upper case” – “A last-resort method of preventing mid-air collisions between aircraft, as directed 
by a Collision Avoidance System.”
• “Lower case” – “A general term referring to the act of maneuvering to prevent mid-air collisions, not 
tied to any specific Collision Avoidance System”
• Collision Avoidance Function – function performed to achieve lower case definition of 
collision avoidance
• Collision Avoidance System – a system that produces directive guidance in the for of 
Resolution Advisories to avoid mid-air collision with other aircraft.
• E.g. TCAS, TCAS II, ACAS X, etc.  - more on these later

Notes on terminology (3)
• More from RTCA DO-365
• Self-Separation – “a system function where the UA maneuvers within a sufficient 
timeframe to remain well clear.”
• Legacy terminology replaced with the more quantitatively based…
• Remain Well Clear (RWC) – “the ability to detect, analyze and maneuver to avoid 
potential conflicting traffic by applying adjustments to the current flight path in 
order to prevent the conflict from developing into a collision hazard”
• Two aspects:
• Maintain DWC
• Regain DWC
• DAA Well Clear (DWC) – a temporal and/or spatial boundary around the aircraft 
intended to be an electronic means of avoiding conflicting traffic.
• We shall discuss what well clear means later in this module.
• Systems like TCAS use some definition of tau (𝜏), an estimate of time to collision.

Notes on terminology (4)
• In DAA terminology, we separate “our” aircraft from other aircraft with the 
following terminology:
• Ownship – in a DAA system, ownship refers to the aircraft that will be performing in 
the detect and avoid functions relative to other aircraft.
• Intruder – in a DAA system, intruder refers to the other aircraft within close enough 
proximity such that it is tracked and potentially avoiding (RWC or CA).

What about segregated airspace?
• The broad consensus from FAA and other CAAs is to avoid implementing UAS or UAM segregated airspace 
•
Avoid showing favoritism to one user class
•
Potentially harmful impact to commercial operations
• NASA’s UAS Traffic Management (UTM) program has explored low-altitude airspace that may be segregated 
for UAS operation
•
Looking at < 400 ft. (primarily)
•
e.g. UAS lanes of travel (vertical and horizontal)
•
Still in early technology readiness level (TRL) – concept phase
•
Must identify how to integrate with other low altitude users:
•
Helicopter
•
Ultralight
•
Model aircraft
•
Etc.
• Advanced Air Mobility (AAM) and Urban Air Mobility (UAM) have similar challenges and receive support 
through Providers of Service for UAM (PSUs) building upon the UTM concepts developed for UAS.
•
No segregation, but coordination for non-UAM to enter UAM corridor and vice versa.

Pilot’s Role
• We cannot assume that the human operator (pilot in command) does not have a role in 
DAA.
• Notional examples:
• Human-in-the-loop:
• Processes sensor inputs
• Command aircraft to performance avoidance
• Human-on-the-loop:
• DAA system detects potential collision threat and notifies pilot
• Pilot approves recommended course of action and/or inputs a corrective action given the alert
• Automated:
• DAA system detects potential collision and takes corrective action
• PIC is notified of the course change (ability to override?)
• Necessary to support contingency operations
• Visual observers may be employed instead of sensors as recommended under Part 107
• Ground-based
• Airborne
• Pilot in command has final legal authority of the actions of the aircraft
• In manned aviation, pilot can choose to justifiably disregard a resolution advisory or ATC 
instruction if they feel it jeopardizes flight safety

Sense / Detect (2)
• Cooperative vs. Non-Cooperative Aircraft
• One major distinction in modalities for detection is whether or not the “intruder” is a 
cooperative aircraft vs. a non-cooperative aircraft
• Cooperative aircraft:
• Equipped with a form of cooperative technology such as:
• Transponder
• ADS-B
• TCAS /ACAS I or II
• Identify, Friend, or Foe System
• Etc.
• Note: more on these later
• Required for operation in controlled airspace / under an instrument flight rules (IFR) flight plan
• Non-cooperative aircraft:
• Not equipped with a technology that aids in detection through traditional air traffic management 
system, transponder system, etc.
• Non-cooperative can utilize other means to help ”be seen”
• Permissible for some aircraft types and/or some airspace categories
• Non-cooperative aircraft are one of the great challenges of UAS integration

“Swiss Cheese” Analogy
• Detect and avoid sensing has been described as working with “swiss 
cheese”
• Sensors and tracking software might have “holes” in its safety 
function.
• Detection limits can include, but are not limited to: range, minimum target 
size, minimum target velocity, altitude of ownship, altitude of intruder, etc.
• A goal of DAA sensor selection is to find the right layers of “cheese” 
such that when stacked together no holes remain.

Review
• A DAA system is designed to ensure that a UAS remains well clear of other 
aircraft, and performs collision avoidance maneuvers if a well clear 
violation occurs or is eminent.
• DAA requirements must be developed to meet the expectations of 14 CFR 
Part 91 to see-and-avoid and follow right-of-way rules within air traffic.
• Part 107 for small UAS relies upon visual observers, but waivers permit 
beyond visual line of sight operations, which require a DAA system
• Elements of the DAA system were summarized with more detail ahead
• Numerous challenges exist with DAA systems including establishing 
performance metrics, defining system requirements, and ensuring 
adequate detection capabilities.
