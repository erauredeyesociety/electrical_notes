## Question 2 — Interpreting QFD (15 pts)

**Why E2 (Motor efficiency) ranks highest.**
Motor efficiency has a *strong* relationship (score 9) with *two* of the three customer requirements — Fast delivery and Reliability — and both carry the highest possible customer importance rating (5). A single engineering lever that addresses two top-priority "whats" accumulates the most weighted votes in the column sum, so it dominates the technical importance ranking. In contrast, Battery capacity only has a strong link to Low cost (importance 4), and Payload capacity has no strong links at all.

**A trade-off revealed by the QFD (roof / correlation matrix).**
A clear negative correlation exists between **Battery capacity (E1)** and **Payload capacity (E3)**: increasing battery size adds weight and volume to the airframe, which directly reduces the payload the drone can carry while still meeting flight endurance. Because Low cost is the only requirement that strongly pulls E1 upward, and E3 has only medium relationships throughout, this is a trade-off the design team should resolve deliberately rather than letting it emerge by accident — probably by holding battery capacity at the minimum required for mission range and freeing the weight budget for payload (or for a more efficient motor, which is already the top priority).

## Question 3 — Integration of Simulation and QFD (15 pts)

Simulation results from the newsvendor-style model (Q4) feed directly back into the QFD by *quantifying* what the relationship matrix only *ranks*. The HoQ says "motor efficiency drives fast delivery and reliability," but the Monte-Carlo simulation tells you *by how much* — specifically, E[W(q)], the probability of loss P(W < 0), and the 95 % confidence interval on profit for a given scheduling policy. If simulation shows high variance in daily profit because demand often exceeds the number of slots the current motor/battery configuration can support, the team has quantitative evidence to either raise E2's target value, raise E2's importance weight, or even add a new customer requirement such as "capacity resilience" to the left column of the HoQ.

**Example.** Suppose simulation shows P(loss) = 22 % at the baseline motor efficiency because under-capacity days cause lost-sale opportunity cost (r − c = $5 per missed delivery). That risk finding could drive a design change to upgrade to a higher-efficiency motor (raising E2's *target*) at the expense of some payload capacity (E3's target lowered) — a trade-off explicitly visible on the HoQ roof. The QFD then re-prioritizes with updated targets and re-runs the simulation to verify that expected profit rises and P(loss) falls below an acceptable threshold.

## Question 4 — Newsvendor Simulation Setup (25 pts)

### a) Profit function

Let `q` = slots scheduled per day, `D` = realized demand (a random variable). With revenue r = \$12 per delivery and cost c = \$7 per slot (and no salvage / scrap value mentioned):

$$
\boxed{\;W(q, D) \;=\; r \cdot \min(q, D) \;-\; c \cdot q \;=\; 12 \cdot \min(q, D) \;-\; 7q\;}
$$

Piecewise view:

- If **D ≥ q** (demand at or above capacity): all slots used → `W = 12q − 7q = 5q` (maximum achievable profit for that q).
- If **D < q** (under-used slots): `W = 12D − 7q`; the `(q − D)` idle slots still cost \$7 each but earn nothing. `W` can go negative.

The break-even demand given q is D* = 7q/12 ≈ 0.583 q; below that realized demand the day loses money.

This follows the lecture formulation **W(q) = r · min(D, q) + s · max(q − D, 0) − c · q** with scrap price s = 0 here (idle slots produce no salvage).

### b) Spreadsheet simulation procedure (no code)

Inputs on a single sheet:

| Cell  | Meaning            | Value    |
|:------|:-------------------|:--------:|
| B4    | Cost per slot (c)  | 7        |
| B5    | Revenue per delivery (r) | 12 |
| B7    | Demand mean (μ)    | e.g. 40  |
| B8    | Demand std. dev. (σ) | e.g. 10 |
| H2    | Slots scheduled (q) — trial values 20, 30, 40, 50, 60 | 40 |

For each of *n* ≥ 30 replications (rows 4 … 3+n), i.e. simulated days:

| Column | Formula |
|:------:|:--------|
| E (Demand D) | `=MAX(ROUND(NORMINV(RAND(), $B$7, $B$8), 0), 0)` |
| F (Sold)     | `=MIN(E4, H$2)` |
| G (Idle slots) | `=MAX(H$2 − E4, 0)`  *(diagnostic; 0 contribution to profit here)* |
| H (Profit W) | `=$B$5*F4 − $B$4*H$2`   →   `=12*F4 − 7*H$2` |

Summary cells below the replications:

- `E[W(q)] = AVERAGE(H4:H33)`
- Std dev `= STDEV(H4:H33)`
- 95 % CI half-width `= 2.045 * STDEV / SQRT(COUNT)`   (t₂₉,₀.₉₇₅ ≈ 2.045)
- `P(loss) = COUNTIF(H4:H33, "<0") / COUNT(H4:H33)`
- Histogram of W to visualize the risk/return distribution.

**How `NORMINV(RAND(), μ, σ)` generates demand.** `RAND()` draws U ~ Uniform(0,1); `NORMINV` applies the inverse normal CDF Φ⁻¹μ,σ(U), giving a normal variate with the specified mean and std. dev. — the inverse-transform method from the lecture. `ROUND(..., 0)` discretizes (whole-number demand); `MAX(..., 0)` truncates any negative variate at zero.

**Sweep and common random numbers.** Put five trial values of q across row 2 (H2, I2, J2, K2, L2 = 20, 30, 40, 50, 60). Each row computes Sold/Profit for every q from the **same** D in column E — so all q columns see identical daily demands (variance reduction via common random numbers, as noted in lecture). Press F9 to re-draw the whole trial. Compare E[W(q)], P(loss), and the 95 % CI across q to pick the best policy.

### c) What happens if q is too large vs. too small?

**q too large (over-schedule).**
Most days have D < q, so the final term 7·q dominates the first term 12·min(q, D). The company pays for idle slots that generate no revenue, and on low-demand days profit can be sharply negative. Simulation signs: E[W(q)] starts falling beyond the optimum, the profit histogram widens, and **P(loss) rises** — higher risk with diminishing return. Overage cost per excess slot = c = \$7.

**q too small (under-schedule).**
On high-demand days the firm can only serve q customers even though D > q, leaving 12 − 7 = \$5 of unit contribution margin on the table for every unmet unit of demand. Profit is more stable (near 5·q every day) but the mean is well below what a larger q could capture. Underage cost per missed sale = r − c = \$5.

**The trade-off.** The optimal q balances the expected cost of idle slots (c per extra unit) against the expected value of lost sales (r − c per missed unit). Larger q shifts the profit distribution *right on average* but also widens it — the lecture framed this as the **risk/return trade-off** (risk-taker vs. risk-averse). Monte-Carlo simulation lets you see both the mean shift and the variance / P(loss) simultaneously, which closed-form newsvendor analysis alone does not show.
