## Question 6 — Essay (10 pts): Dominated Strategies

**Payoff table (Player 1's payoffs):**

| P1 \ P2 |  1 |  2 |  3 |
|:-------:|:--:|:--:|:--:|
| **1**   |  1 |  2 |  4 |
| **2**   |  1 |  0 |  5 |
| **3**   |  0 |  1 | -1 |

### a) Dominated strategies for Player 1

Compare each row against every other row (Player 1 maximizes, so row A dominates row B if every entry of A ≥ corresponding entry of B, with at least one strict inequality).

- **Row 1 vs Row 3:** (1, 2, 4) vs (0, 1, -1) → 1 > 0, 2 > 1, 4 > -1. **Row 1 strictly dominates Row 3.**
- Row 1 vs Row 2: (1, 2, 4) vs (1, 0, 5) → col 3 favors Row 2, so neither dominates.
- Row 2 vs Row 3: (1, 0, 5) vs (0, 1, -1) → col 2 favors Row 3, so neither dominates.

**Result:** Row 3 is dominated (by Row 1). Player 1 should never play strategy 3.

### b) Remove Row 3; does a Player 2 strategy become dominated?

Reduced table:

| P1 \ P2 |  1 |  2 |  3 |
|:-------:|:--:|:--:|:--:|
| **1**   |  1 |  2 |  4 |
| **2**   |  1 |  0 |  5 |

Player 2 *minimizes* Player 1's payoff, so column A dominates column B (for P2) if every entry of A ≤ corresponding entry of B.

- Col 1 (1, 1) vs Col 3 (4, 5) → 1 < 4 and 1 < 5. **Col 1 strictly dominates Col 3.**
- Col 2 (2, 0) vs Col 3 (4, 5) → 2 < 4 and 0 < 5. **Col 2 also strictly dominates Col 3.**

**Result:** After eliminating Row 3, Player 2's strategy 3 becomes dominated (by both strategies 1 and 2). Player 2 should never play strategy 3.

Final 2×2 reduced game:

| P1 \ P2 |  1 |  2 |
|:-------:|:--:|:--:|
| **1**   |  1 |  2 |
| **2**   |  1 |  0 |

## Question 7 — Essay (25 pts): Maximin / Minimax / Saddle Point

**Payoff table (Player 1's payoffs):**

| P1 \ P2 |  1 |  2 |  3 |
|:-------:|:--:|:--:|:--:|
| **1**   | -3 | -2 |  6 |
| **2**   |  2 |  0 |  2 |
| **3**   |  5 | -2 | -4 |

### a) Row minima (worst case for each Player 1 strategy)

| Row | Entries        | **Row min** |
|:---:|:---------------|:-----------:|
|  1  | −3, −2, 6      |   **−3**    |
|  2  |  2,  0, 2      |  **  0**    |
|  3  |  5, −2, −4     |   **−4**    |

### b) Maximin strategy for Player 1

Maximin = max(−3, 0, −4) = **0**, attained at **Row 2**.
→ **Player 1 plays strategy 2.**

### c) Column maxima (worst case for Player 2, who wants small numbers)

| Col | Entries          | **Col max** |
|:---:|:-----------------|:-----------:|
|  1  | −3, 2, 5         |   **5**     |
|  2  | −2, 0, −2        |   **0**     |
|  3  |  6, 2, −4        |   **6**     |

### d) Minimax strategy for Player 2

Minimax = min(5, 0, 6) = **0**, attained at **Column 2**.
→ **Player 2 plays strategy 2.**

### e) Saddle point?

Maximin (0) = Minimax (0), so **a saddle point exists** at cell **(P1 = 2, P2 = 2)** with value **v = 0**.
Check: the entry `0` is the minimum of row 2 *and* the maximum of column 2. ✓

**Value of the game = 0** (fair game). Both players have an optimal pure strategy; neither can unilaterally improve.

## Question 8 — Essay (10 pts): Expected Payoff Under Mixed Strategies

**Given:**
- `x = (x₁, x₂, x₃) = (0.5, 0.5, 0)`
- `y = (y₁, y₂, y₃) = (0, 0.5, 0.5)`

**Payoff table (Player 1's payoffs):**

| P1 \ P2 |  1 |  2 |  3 |
|:-------:|:--:|:--:|:--:|
| **1**   |  0 | −2 |  2 |
| **2**   |  5 |  4 | −3 |
| **3**   |  2 |  3 | −4 |

### Formula

$$
E[\text{payoff}] \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{3}\sum_{j=1}^{3} x_i \, y_j \, a_{ij}
$$

Only four terms survive (the ones where both `xᵢ` and `yⱼ` are nonzero): (i,j) ∈ {(1,2), (1,3), (2,2), (2,3)}.

### Step by step

| Term        | `xᵢ · yⱼ`      | `aᵢⱼ` | Contribution      |
|:-----------:|:--------------:|:-----:|:-----------------:|
| (1, 2)      | 0.5·0.5 = 0.25 |  −2   |  0.25 · (−2) = **−0.50** |
| (1, 3)      | 0.5·0.5 = 0.25 |   2   |  0.25 · ( 2) = **+0.50** |
| (2, 2)      | 0.5·0.5 = 0.25 |   4   |  0.25 · ( 4) = **+1.00** |
| (2, 3)      | 0.5·0.5 = 0.25 |  −3   |  0.25 · (−3) = **−0.75** |

**Sum:** −0.50 + 0.50 + 1.00 − 0.75 = **0.25**

### Result

$$
\boxed{\;E[\text{payoff to Player 1}] \;=\; 0.25\;}
$$

## Question 9 — Essay (20 pts): Classify the Games

> Definitions
> - **Zero-sum:** one player's gain exactly equals the other's loss (sum of payoffs = 0).
> - **Constant-sum:** payoffs always sum to the same constant (strategically equivalent to zero-sum).
> - **Nonzero-sum:** total payoff varies across outcomes; cooperation can create (or destroy) value.

### A. Two firms competing for the same customers
**Constant-sum (market share) — or nonzero-sum in a growing market.**
If the customer pool is fixed, every customer one firm gains the other loses, so the shares sum to a constant (100%). This is strategically zero-sum. When the overall market can grow or shrink because of advertising, price wars, or innovation, total profit is no longer fixed and the game becomes nonzero-sum.

### B. Two countries negotiating trade restrictions
**Nonzero-sum.**
Both countries can be better off with mutual free trade (cooperation creates surplus) and both worse off with mutual tariffs (deadweight loss). Total welfare depends on the outcome, so payoffs do not sum to a constant. This is the classic cooperative/bargaining structure.

### C. A penalty kick in soccer
**Zero-sum.**
The kicker's gain (goal scored) is exactly the goalkeeper's loss, and vice versa. Payoffs can be coded as +1 / −1 and always sum to zero. It is a pure strategic conflict — the textbook example for mixed-strategy equilibrium.

### D. Two roommates deciding how to share house chores
**Nonzero-sum.**
Both benefit from a clean house, and cooperation (splitting chores fairly) creates more total welfare than either doing nothing. The outcome is not a pure transfer between the players — the "size of the pie" depends on the strategy profile.

## Question 10 — Essay (10 pts): Prisoner's Dilemma Paragraph

The Prisoner's Dilemma illustrates the tension between individual rationality and collective benefit because each player has a **dominant strategy to defect** even though **mutual cooperation** would leave both players strictly better off. Formally, defecting yields a higher payoff for a player regardless of what the opponent does, so rational self-interest drives both toward the (Defect, Defect) outcome — a Nash equilibrium that is **Pareto-inferior** to (Cooperate, Cooperate). The paradox is that two rational agents, each optimizing individually, collectively arrive at a worse result than if they had coordinated.

A clear example outside criminal justice is **environmental policy and carbon emissions**. Each country faces a choice between cutting emissions (cooperate — incurring short-term economic costs) or continuing to pollute (defect — preserving short-term growth). No matter what other countries do, any single country gains more economically by continuing to pollute, so defection is individually rational. But if every country reasons the same way, all of them suffer the long-term consequences of climate change, a collectively catastrophic outcome that leaves everyone worse off than universal cooperation would have. This mirrors the Prisoner's Dilemma exactly: the Nash equilibrium (everyone pollutes) is strictly dominated in welfare terms by the unreachable cooperative outcome (everyone cuts emissions), which is why international climate agreements, binding treaties, and enforcement mechanisms are needed — they change the payoff structure so that cooperation becomes individually rational as well as collectively beneficial.
