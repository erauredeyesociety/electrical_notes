# Chapter 15 — Mechanical Waves

## 1. Types of Mechanical Waves

A **mechanical wave** is a disturbance that travels through a material called the **medium**. Two basic kinds:

- **Transverse waves** — particles move perpendicular to the direction of propagation (e.g., wave on a string).
- **Longitudinal waves** — particles move parallel/anti-parallel to the direction of propagation (e.g., sound).

Waves on the surface of a liquid combine both (surface particles move in circles).

Waves propagate through a medium at a definite speed called the **wave speed**. Particles sustaining the motion move in **simple harmonic motion** about their equilibrium points. Although no net matter is transported, **energy** is transported from one region to another.

## 2. Periodic Waves

Characteristic properties:
- $A$ = amplitude (m)
- $f$ = frequency (Hz)
- $T$ = period (s)
- $\lambda$ = wavelength (m)

**Harmonic waves** are sinusoidal. For periodic waves:

$$v = f\lambda$$

### 2.1 Transverse Waves
Particles move perpendicular to the propagation direction. Generated by shaking a taut string up and down. Electromagnetic waves are transverse and can travel in vacuum.

### 2.2 Longitudinal Waves
Particles move parallel to the propagation direction. Sound is longitudinal.

## 3. Mathematical Description of a Wave

### 3.1 Wave Function for a Harmonic Wave

For a sinusoidal wave traveling left-to-right:

$$y(x, t) = A\cos\!\left[2\pi f\!\left(\frac{x}{v} - t\right)\right] = A\cos\!\left[2\pi\!\left(\frac{x}{\lambda} - \frac{t}{T}\right)\right]$$

More convenient form:

$$y(x, t) = A\cos(kx - \omega t) \quad \text{(left-to-right)}$$

where $k = 2\pi/\lambda$ is the **wave number** and $\omega = 2\pi f$ is the **angular frequency**.

For a wave moving right-to-left, change the sign:

$$y(x, t) = A\sin(kx + \omega t) \quad \text{(right-to-left)}$$

**Example 8**: Given $y(x,t) = (6.50~\mathrm{mm})\cos 2\pi\!\left(\frac{x}{28.0~\mathrm{cm}} - \frac{t}{0.0360~\mathrm{s}}\right)$. Read off: $A = 6.5~\mathrm{mm}$, $\lambda = 28.0~\mathrm{cm}$, $T = 0.036~\mathrm{s}$, $f = 1/T$, $v = \lambda/T$, direction = $+x$.

### 3.2 Particle Velocity and Acceleration

For a wave $y(x, t) = A\cos(kx - \omega t)$:

$$v_y(x, t) = \frac{\partial y}{\partial t} = \omega A \sin(kx - \omega t)$$

$$a_y(x, t) = \frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial t^2} = -\omega^2 A \cos(kx - \omega t)$$

### 3.3 The Wave Equation

$$\frac{\partial^2 y(x,t)}{\partial x^2} = -k^2 y(x,t)$$

$$\frac{\partial^2 y(x,t)}{\partial t^2} = -\omega^2 y(x,t)$$

Combining with $v = \omega/k$:

$$\boxed{\frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial x^2} = \frac{1}{v^2}\frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial t^2}} \quad \text{(Wave Equation)}$$

This equation holds for other wave functions too — e.g., $y = A/(x - vt)^2$.

## 4. Speed of a Transverse Wave

Apply Newton's second law to a string segment of length $\Delta x$ and mass $\mu \Delta x$. For small transverse displacements:

$$\frac{F_{1y}}{F} = -\left(\frac{\partial y}{\partial x}\right)_x, \quad \frac{F_{2y}}{F} = \left(\frac{\partial y}{\partial x}\right)_{x+\Delta x}$$

Net vertical force:

$$F_y = F\left[\left(\frac{\partial y}{\partial x}\right)_{x+\Delta x} - \left(\frac{\partial y}{\partial x}\right)_x\right] = (\mu\Delta x)\frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial t^2}$$

Taking $\Delta x \to 0$:

$$\frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial x^2} = \frac{\mu}{F}\frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial t^2}$$

Comparing with the wave equation:

$$\boxed{v = \sqrt{\frac{F}{\mu}}} \quad \text{(velocity of transverse wave on a string)}$$

**Example 15**: Rope attached to a tuning fork at 120 Hz; other end passes over a pulley supporting a 1.50-kg mass; $\mu = 0.0480~\mathrm{kg/m}$.
(a) $v = \sqrt{F/\mu} = \sqrt{(1.5)(9.8)/0.048}$.
(b) $\lambda = v/f$.
(c) For $m = 3.00~\mathrm{kg}$: $v$ increases by $\sqrt{2}$, $\lambda$ too.

## 5. Energy in Wave Motion

For a transverse wave, the $y$-component of force is proportional to the negative of the slope:

$$F_y(x, t) = -F\frac{\partial y(x,t)}{\partial x}$$

Instantaneous power:

$$P_\mathrm{inst} = F_y(x,t)\,v_y(x,t) = -F\frac{\partial y}{\partial x}\frac{\partial y}{\partial t}$$

For a sinusoidal wave $y = A\cos(kx - \omega t)$:

$$P(x, t) = Fk\omega A^2 \sin^2(kx - \omega t) = \sqrt{\mu F}\,\omega^2 A^2 \sin^2(kx - \omega t)$$

Maximum and average power:

$$P_\max = \sqrt{\mu F}\,\omega^2 A^2, \quad P_\mathrm{av} = \tfrac{1}{2}\sqrt{\mu F}\,\omega^2 A^2$$

> Figure: Wave power versus time at $x = 0$; oscillates between 0 and $P_\max$ at period $T$; time average is $P_\max/2$.

**Example 22**: Piano wire, $m = 3.00~\mathrm{g}$, $L = 0.80~\mathrm{m}$, tension 25.0 N, $f = 120~\mathrm{Hz}$, $A = 1.6~\mathrm{mm}$.
(a) $\mu = m/L$, $P_\mathrm{av} = \tfrac{1}{2}\sqrt{\mu F}\omega^2 A^2$.
(b) Halving $A$ reduces average power by factor of 4.

### 5.1 Wave Intensity

For waves spreading in three dimensions:

$$I = \frac{\text{Power}}{\text{Area}} = \frac{P}{4\pi R^2}$$

Units: $\mathrm{W/m^2}$. This gives the inverse-square law: $I_1/I_2 = r_2^2/r_1^2$.

## 6. Wave Interference, Boundary Conditions, and Superposition

When a wave meets a boundary, it reflects:
- **Fixed-end boundary**: reflected wave is **inverted**.
- **Free-end boundary**: reflected wave is **not inverted**.

### 6.1 The Principle of Superposition

$$y(x, t) = y_1(x, t) + y_2(x, t)$$

When two waves overlap, the actual displacement at any point is the sum of the individual displacements.

**Example 30 — Interference of Triangular Pulses**: Two identical triangular pulses travel toward each other at 2.00 cm/s on a stretched string. Leading edges 1.00 cm apart at $t = 0$. Sketch the string at $t = 0.250, 0.500, 0.750, 1.000, 1.250~\mathrm{s}$.

## 7. Standing Waves on a String

Superposition of two traveling waves with the same amplitude, frequency, and wavelength moving in opposite directions:

$$y_1 = A\cos(kx - \omega t), \quad y_2 = -A\cos(kx + \omega t)$$

$$y(x, t) = y_1 + y_2 = (2A\sin kx)\sin\omega t$$

Defining $A_\mathrm{SW} = 2A$:

$$\boxed{y(x, t) = (A_\mathrm{SW}\sin kx)\sin\omega t} \quad \text{(Standing Wave)}$$

**Nodes** (points with zero motion) occur where $\sin kx = 0$:

$$x = 0,\ \tfrac{\lambda}{2},\ \tfrac{2\lambda}{2},\ \tfrac{3\lambda}{2},\ \ldots$$

**Example 35**: Standing wave with $A_\mathrm{SW} = 2.50~\mathrm{mm}$, $\omega = 942~\mathrm{rad/s}$, $k = 0.750\pi~\mathrm{rad/m}$, left end at $x = 0$. Find (a) nodes and (b) antinodes.

## 8. Normal Modes of a String

For a string of length $L$ fixed at both ends, standing waves occur only when:

$$L = n\frac{\lambda}{2}, \quad \lambda_n = \frac{2L}{n}, \quad (n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots)$$

| $n$ | Name            | Harmonic      |
|-----|-----------------|---------------|
| 1   | fundamental     | 1st harmonic  |
| 2   | 1st overtone    | 2nd harmonic  |
| 3   | 2nd overtone    | 3rd harmonic  |
| 4   | 3rd overtone    | 4th harmonic  |

Using $v = f_n\lambda_n$:

$$f_n = \frac{nv}{2L} = n f_1, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$$

The possible standing-wave wave functions are:

$$y_n(x, t) = A_\mathrm{SW}\sin(k_n x)\sin(\omega_n t)$$

with $k_n = 2\pi/\lambda_n$ and $\omega_n = 2\pi f_n$.

If $F$ and $\mu$ are known:

$$\boxed{f_n = \frac{n}{2L}\sqrt{\frac{F}{\mu}}} \quad \text{(standing waves on a fixed-length string)}$$

**Example 38**: Steel piano wire, $F = 800~\mathrm{N}$, $L = 0.400~\mathrm{m}$, $m = 3.00~\mathrm{g}$. (a) Fundamental frequency. (b) Highest harmonic audible (≤10 000 Hz).

**Example 47 — Guitar String**: $L = 63.5~\mathrm{cm}$, fundamental tuned to B$_3$ (245 Hz). (a) Wave speed. (b) New $f_1$ if tension increases by 1.0%. (c) Frequency and wavelength of sound produced in air ($v_\mathrm{sound} = 344~\mathrm{m/s}$).
