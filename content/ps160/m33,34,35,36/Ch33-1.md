## 1. Introduction

Optics explains blue skies, rainbows, lens design, lasers, and imaging. This chapter explores reflection, refraction, dispersion, polarization, and scattering, and introduces Huygens's principle.

## 2. The Nature of Light

Historically light was thought to be particles (corpuscles), then established as waves via Maxwell's equations and Hertz's experiments. Modern view (since ~1930 QED): dual wave-particle nature.

**Key constant:** speed of light in vacuum
$$c \approx 3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$$
(defined by cesium clock).

### 2.1 The Two Personalities of Light

EM radiation originates from accelerating electric charges (thermal emission, discharges, lasers). It always travels at $c$ in vacuum.

### 2.2 Waves, Wave Fronts, and Rays

A **wave front** is the locus of points with the same phase. Point sources emit spherical wave fronts; far away they become **plane waves**.

A **ray** is an imaginary line along the direction of wave propagation, normal to the wave front in homogeneous media.

> Figure: Spherical wave fronts expanding from a point source; rays radiate outward. For plane waves, rays are parallel and perpendicular to the flat wave fronts.

## 3. Reflection and Refraction

When light strikes a smooth interface between two transparent materials, it is partly reflected and partly refracted (transmitted).

- **Specular reflection:** smooth surface → well-defined reflected angle.
- **Diffuse reflection:** rough surface → scattered rays. Most objects are visible via diffuse reflection.

### Index of Refraction

$$n = \frac{c}{v}$$
where $v$ is the speed of light in the material. $n \geq 1$ for all materials; $n = 1$ exactly for vacuum, $\approx 1.0003$ for air. Depends on wavelength (→ dispersion).

**Selected indices of refraction (yellow sodium light, $\lambda_0 = 589$ nm):**

| Substance | $n$ |
|-----------|-----|
| Ice (H₂O) | 1.309 |
| Water (20°C) | 1.333 |
| Ethanol | 1.36 |
| Glycerine | 1.473 |
| Crown glass | 1.52 |
| Light flint glass | 1.58 |
| Quartz (SiO₂) | 1.544 |
| Diamond (C) | 2.417 |
| Rutile (TiO₂) | 2.62 |

### 3.1 The Laws of Reflection and Refraction

The incident ray, reflected ray, refracted ray, and normal all lie in the same plane (plane of incidence). All angles measured **from the normal**.

**Law of reflection:**
$$\theta_r = \theta_a$$

**Snell's law (law of refraction):**
$$n_a \sin\theta_a = n_b \sin\theta_b$$

> Figure: Incident/reflected/refracted rays at an interface, with normals. When light enters a higher-$n$ medium, it bends **toward** the normal; when entering a lower-$n$ medium, it bends **away**.

### 3.2 Index of Refraction and the Wave Aspects of Light

Crossing an interface:
- **Frequency** $f$ is unchanged (boundary cannot create/destroy waves).
- **Wavelength** changes:
$$\lambda = \frac{\lambda_0}{n}$$
where $\lambda_0$ is the vacuum wavelength. Higher $n$ → shorter wavelength in the medium.
- Speed: $v = \lambda f$, so $v$ decreases as $n$ increases.

## 4. Total Internal Reflection

Occurs when light travels from higher to lower index and angle of incidence exceeds the **critical angle**. At the critical angle, refracted angle = 90°.

$$\sin\theta_{\text{crit}} = \frac{n_b}{n_a} \quad (n_b < n_a)$$

For $\theta_a > \theta_{\text{crit}}$, all light is reflected back into the denser medium.

**Fiber optics** exploit this: light trapped in a transparent rod (core) with higher $n$ than cladding, bouncing along even in curved paths.

> Figure: Rays 1, 2 refract out; ray 3 hits at critical angle ($\theta_b = 90°$); ray 4 undergoes total internal reflection.

## 5. Dispersion

Ordinary white light is a superposition of all visible wavelengths. In vacuum all travel at $c$, but in materials wave speed (and hence $n$) depends on wavelength. This variation is called **dispersion**.

In most materials $n$ **decreases** with increasing $\lambda$ (decreasing $f$). Hence violet bends more than red in a prism — giving the spectrum.

> Figure: Prism disperses white light into a rainbow spectrum (violet refracted most, red least).

### Rainbows

Primary rainbow: single internal reflection inside raindrops; $\Delta \approx 40.8°$ (violet) to $42.5°$ (red). Secondary rainbow: two internal reflections; colors reversed; $\approx 50.1°$ (red) to $53.2°$ (violet).

## 6. Polarization

Polarization is a property of **transverse waves**. An EM wave is **linearly polarized** if $\vec{E}$ oscillates in a single direction perpendicular to propagation.

Most light sources emit **unpolarized** (natural) light — a random mixture of polarizations. A **polarizing filter** (Polaroid) transmits only components of $\vec{E}$ parallel to its axis.

**Malus's law:** When linearly polarized light of intensity $I_{\max}$ passes through an analyzer at angle $\phi$ from the polarization direction:
$$I = I_{\max}\cos^2\phi$$

For unpolarized light passing through a single polarizer, intensity drops to $I_0/2$.

### Polarization by Reflection — Brewster's Law

At one special angle of incidence $\theta_p$ (Brewster's angle), the reflected light is **completely polarized** perpendicular to the plane of incidence. At this angle the reflected and refracted rays are perpendicular, and:
$$\tan\theta_p = \frac{n_b}{n_a}$$

### Circular and Elliptical Polarization

Superposing two linearly polarized waves 90° out of phase produces **circular polarization** (constant $|\vec{E}|$ rotating). A quarter-wave plate converts linear ↔ circular polarization. 3-D movie glasses use circular polarizing filters.

## 7. Scattering of Light

Light scattered by atmospheric molecules is partially polarized. Intensity of scattered light is proportional to $f^4$ (or $1/\lambda^4$) — hence blue light scatters ~15× more than red, giving the **blue sky**. Sunsets are red because blue is scattered away along the long atmospheric path. Clouds appear white because droplets are much larger than $\lambda$ and scatter all wavelengths equally.

## 8. Huygens's Principle

**Huygens's principle:** Every point on a wave front acts as a source of secondary spherical wavelets that spread out with speed $v$. The envelope of these wavelets at a later time forms the new wave front.

Using Huygens's principle, both the law of reflection and Snell's law can be derived from the geometry of wavelets at an interface:
$$n_a \sin\theta_a = n_b \sin\theta_b$$

> Figure: Huygens's principle applied to wave front $AA'$ generates wavelets of radius $r = vt$ whose envelope is the next wave front $BB'$.

## Summary of Key Equations — Chapter 33

- Index of refraction: $n = c/v$
- Law of reflection: $\theta_r = \theta_a$
- Snell's law: $n_a \sin\theta_a = n_b \sin\theta_b$
- Wavelength in medium: $\lambda = \lambda_0/n$
- Critical angle (TIR): $\sin\theta_{\text{crit}} = n_b/n_a$
- Malus's law: $I = I_{\max}\cos^2\phi$
- Brewster's angle: $\tan\theta_p = n_b/n_a$
- Speed of light: $c \approx 3.00\times 10^8$ m/s
