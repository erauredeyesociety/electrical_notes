      PATH A: Applying KVL/KCL in s-Domain

      Overview

      Once you have your s-domain circuit (from 04-path-a-time-to-sdomain.md), you apply Kirchhoff’s laws exactly as you would in DC circuit analysis - but now you’re working with impedances and complex frequency s.

        graph TD
    A[s-Domain Circuit<br/>with Impedances] --> B{What are you solving for?}
    B -->|Voltage or Series| C[Use KVL<br/>Mesh/Loop Analysis]
    B -->|Current or Parallel| D[Use KCL<br/>Node Analysis]
    
    C --> E[Algebraic Equation in s]
    D --> E

      Kirchhoff’s Voltage Law (KVL) in s-Domain

      Basic Principle

      KVL: The sum of voltages around any closed loop equals zero.

      ∑Vrises(s)−∑Vdrops(s)=0\sum V_{rises}(s) - \sum V_{drops}(s) = 0∑Vrises​(s)−∑Vdrops​(s)=0

      Or equivalently:

      ∑Vsources(s)=∑Vdrops(s)\sum V_{sources}(s) = \sum V_{drops}(s)∑Vsources​(s)=∑Vdrops​(s)

      Voltage Drop Across Impedances

      In s-domain, Ohm’s Law applies:

      V(s)=I(s)⋅Z(s)V(s) = I(s) \cdot Z(s)V(s)=I(s)⋅Z(s)

      Where:

      • V(s) = voltage across impedance
      • I(s) = current through impedance
      • Z(s) = impedance value

      When to Use KVL

      Use KVL for:

      • Series circuits (elements share same current)
      • Mesh/loop analysis
      • When solving for current
      • Circuits with few loops

      KVL Step-by-Step Process

      Step 1: Choose Loop Direction

      Pick clockwise or counterclockwise - stick with it.

      Step 2: Mark Current Direction

      Draw current I(s) flowing around the loop.

      Step 3: Write Voltage Terms

      For each component, going in loop direction:

      • Voltage source: + if going − to +, − if going + to −
      • Impedance: Use passive sign convention
        • Voltage drop = I(s) \times Z(s) in direction of current

      Step 4: Apply KVL Equation

      Vsource(s)=I(s)⋅Z1(s)+I(s)⋅Z2(s)+⋯V_{source}(s) = I(s) \cdot Z_1(s) + I(s) \cdot Z_2(s) + \cdotsVsource​(s)=I(s)⋅Z1​(s)+I(s)⋅Z2​(s)+⋯

      Factor out I(s):

      Vsource(s)=I(s)[Z1(s)+Z2(s)+⋯ ]V_{source}(s) = I(s)[Z_1(s) + Z_2(s) + \cdots]Vsource​(s)=I(s)[Z1​(s)+Z2​(s)+⋯]

      KVL Example 1: Series RC Circuit

      s-Domain Circuit 

      V_s(s) = 10/s ----[R=100Ω]----[Z_C=10⁵/s Ω]---- ground
                   ← I(s) →

      Apply KVL (clockwise)

      Going around loop:

      Vs(s)−I(s)⋅R−I(s)⋅ZC=0V_s(s) - I(s) \cdot R - I(s) \cdot Z_C = 0Vs​(s)−I(s)⋅R−I(s)⋅ZC​=0Vs(s)=I(s)⋅R+I(s)⋅ZCV_s(s) = I(s) \cdot R + I(s) \cdot Z_CVs​(s)=I(s)⋅R+I(s)⋅ZC​10s=I(s)[100+105s]\frac{10}{s} = I(s)[100 + \frac{10^5}{s}]s10​=I(s)[100+s105​]

      Common denominator:

      10s=I(s)[100s+105s]\frac{10}{s} = I(s)\left[\frac{100s + 10^5}{s}\right]s10​=I(s)[s100s+105​]

      Solve for I(s):

      I(s)=10s⋅s100s+105=10100s+105I(s) = \frac{10}{s} \cdot \frac{s}{100s + 10^5} = \frac{10}{100s + 10^5}I(s)=s10​⋅100s+105s​=100s+10510​

      Simplify:

      I(s)=10100(s+1000)=0.1s+1000I(s) = \frac{10}{100(s + 1000)} = \frac{0.1}{s + 1000}I(s)=100(s+1000)10​=s+10000.1​

      Find Voltage Across Capacitor

      VC(s)=I(s)⋅ZC=0.1s+1000⋅105sV_C(s) = I(s) \cdot Z_C = \frac{0.1}{s+1000} \cdot \frac{10^5}{s}VC​(s)=I(s)⋅ZC​=s+10000.1​⋅s105​VC(s)=104s(s+1000)V_C(s) = \frac{10^4}{s(s+1000)}VC​(s)=s(s+1000)104​

      KVL Example 2: Series RL Circuit

      s-Domain Circuit 

      V_s(s) = 5/(s+2) ----[R=10Ω]----[Z_L=0.5s Ω]---- ground
                        ← I(s) →

      Apply KVL

      Vs(s)=I(s)⋅R+I(s)⋅ZLV_s(s) = I(s) \cdot R + I(s) \cdot Z_LVs​(s)=I(s)⋅R+I(s)⋅ZL​5s+2=I(s)[10+0.5s]\frac{5}{s+2} = I(s)[10 + 0.5s]s+25​=I(s)[10+0.5s]

      Solve for I(s):

      I(s)=5(s+2)(10+0.5s)I(s) = \frac{5}{(s+2)(10 + 0.5s)}I(s)=(s+2)(10+0.5s)5​I(s)=50.5(s+2)(20+s)=10(s+2)(s+20)I(s) = \frac{5}{0.5(s+2)(20+s)} = \frac{10}{(s+2)(s+20)}I(s)=0.5(s+2)(20+s)5​=(s+2)(s+20)10​

      Kirchhoff’s Current Law (KCL) in s-Domain

      Basic Principle

      KCL: The sum of currents entering a node equals the sum leaving.

      ∑Iin(s)=∑Iout(s)\sum I_{in}(s) = \sum I_{out}(s)∑Iin​(s)=∑Iout​(s)

      Or:

      ∑Isources(s)=∑Ibranches(s)\sum I_{sources}(s) = \sum I_{branches}(s)∑Isources​(s)=∑Ibranches​(s)

      Current Through Impedances

      Using Ohm’s Law:

      I(s)=V(s)Z(s)I(s) = \frac{V(s)}{Z(s)}I(s)=Z(s)V(s)​

      Where V(s) is the voltage across the impedance.

      When to Use KCL

      Use KCL for:

      • Parallel circuits (elements share same voltage)
      • Node analysis
      • When solving for voltage
      • Circuits with few nodes

      KCL Step-by-Step Process

      Step 1: Identify Node

      Choose the node where branches meet.

      Step 2: Define Node Voltage

      Let V(s) = voltage at the node (relative to ground).

      Step 3: Write Current Terms

      For each branch connected to node:

      Ibranch(s)=Vnode(s)Zbranch(s)I_{branch}(s) = \frac{V_{node}(s)}{Z_{branch}(s)}Ibranch​(s)=Zbranch​(s)Vnode​(s)​

      Step 4: Apply KCL Equation

      Isource(s)=∑V(s)Zi(s)I_{source}(s) = \sum \frac{V(s)}{Z_i(s)}Isource​(s)=∑Zi​(s)V(s)​

      Factor out V(s):

      Isource(s)=V(s)∑1Zi(s)I_{source}(s) = V(s) \sum \frac{1}{Z_i(s)}Isource​(s)=V(s)∑Zi​(s)1​

      KCL Example 1: Parallel RL Circuit

      s-Domain Circuit 

               I_s(s) = 2/(s+3)
              |
         +----V(s)----+
         |            |
        R=4Ω         Z_L=2s Ω
         |            |
       ground       ground

      Apply KCL at Top Node

      Currents leaving node:

      Is(s)=IR(s)+IL(s)I_s(s) = I_R(s) + I_L(s)Is​(s)=IR​(s)+IL​(s)

      Express in terms of V(s):

      • IR(s)=V(s)R=V(s)4I_R(s) = \frac{V(s)}{R} = \frac{V(s)}{4}IR​(s)=RV(s)​=4V(s)​
      • IL(s)=V(s)ZL=V(s)2sI_L(s) = \frac{V(s)}{Z_L} = \frac{V(s)}{2s}IL​(s)=ZL​V(s)​=2sV(s)​

      KCL equation:

      2s+3=V(s)4+V(s)2s\frac{2}{s+3} = \frac{V(s)}{4} + \frac{V(s)}{2s}s+32​=4V(s)​+2sV(s)​

      Factor out V(s):

      2s+3=V(s)[14+12s]\frac{2}{s+3} = V(s)\left[\frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{2s}\right]s+32​=V(s)[41​+2s1​]

      Common denominator:

      2s+3=V(s)[s+24s]\frac{2}{s+3} = V(s)\left[\frac{s + 2}{4s}\right]s+32​=V(s)[4ss+2​]

      Solve for V(s):

      V(s)=2s+3⋅4ss+2=8s(s+3)(s+2)V(s) = \frac{2}{s+3} \cdot \frac{4s}{s+2} = \frac{8s}{(s+3)(s+2)}V(s)=s+32​⋅s+24s​=(s+3)(s+2)8s​

      KCL Example 2: Parallel RLC Circuit

      s-Domain Circuit 

               I_s(s) = 4/s
              |
         +----V(s)----+----+----+
         |            |    |    |
        R=5Ω        Z_L  Z_C
         |          =2s  =10/s
       ground      gnd  gnd

      Apply KCL at Top Node

      Is(s)=IR(s)+IL(s)+IC(s)I_s(s) = I_R(s) + I_L(s) + I_C(s)Is​(s)=IR​(s)+IL​(s)+IC​(s)

      Express in terms of V(s):

      • IR(s)=V(s)5I_R(s) = \frac{V(s)}{5}IR​(s)=5V(s)​
      • IL(s)=V(s)2sI_L(s) = \frac{V(s)}{2s}IL​(s)=2sV(s)​
      • IC(s)=V(s)⋅s10I_C(s) = V(s) \cdot \frac{s}{10}IC​(s)=V(s)⋅10s​ (since Z_C = 10/s, so 1/Z_C = s/10)

      KCL equation:

      4s=V(s)5+V(s)2s+sV(s)10\frac{4}{s} = \frac{V(s)}{5} + \frac{V(s)}{2s} + \frac{sV(s)}{10}s4​=5V(s)​+2sV(s)​+10sV(s)​

      Factor out V(s):

      4s=V(s)[15+12s+s10]\frac{4}{s} = V(s)\left[\frac{1}{5} + \frac{1}{2s} + \frac{s}{10}\right]s4​=V(s)[51​+2s1​+10s​]

      Common denominator (10s):

      4s=V(s)[2s+5+s210s]\frac{4}{s} = V(s)\left[\frac{2s + 5 + s^2}{10s}\right]s4​=V(s)[10s2s+5+s2​]4s=V(s)[s2+2s+510s]\frac{4}{s} = V(s)\left[\frac{s^2 + 2s + 5}{10s}\right]s4​=V(s)[10ss2+2s+5​]

      Solve for V(s):

      V(s)=4s⋅10ss2+2s+5=40s2+2s+5V(s) = \frac{4}{s} \cdot \frac{10s}{s^2 + 2s + 5} = \frac{40}{s^2 + 2s + 5}V(s)=s4​⋅s2+2s+510s​=s2+2s+540​

      Mixed Analysis: Series-Parallel Circuits

      For complex topologies, you may need to combine methods.

      Approach 1: Simplify First

      Combine series/parallel impedances before applying KVL/KCL.

      Example: If R₂ and C are in parallel within a series circuit:

      1. Find Z_parallel = (R₂ \times Z_C)/(R₂ + Z_C)
      2. Now circuit is series: R₁ with Z_parallel
      3. Apply KVL

      Approach 2: Multiple Equations

      Write separate KVL/KCL for each loop/node, then solve system.

      Approach 3: Node-Voltage Method

      Choose one analysis method and stick with it throughout.

      Key Equations Summary

      For Series Circuits (KVL)

      Total impedance:

      Ztotal=Z1+Z2+⋯+ZnZ_{total} = Z_1 + Z_2 + \cdots + Z_nZtotal​=Z1​+Z2​+⋯+Zn​

      Current:

      I(s)=Vs(s)ZtotalI(s) = \frac{V_s(s)}{Z_{total}}I(s)=Ztotal​Vs​(s)​

      Voltage across element i:

      Vi(s)=I(s)⋅Zi(s)V_i(s) = I(s) \cdot Z_i(s)Vi​(s)=I(s)⋅Zi​(s)

      For Parallel Circuits (KCL)

      Total admittance:

      Ytotal=Y1+Y2+⋯+Yn=1Z1+1Z2+⋯Y_{total} = Y_1 + Y_2 + \cdots + Y_n = \frac{1}{Z_1} + \frac{1}{Z_2} + \cdotsYtotal​=Y1​+Y2​+⋯+Yn​=Z1​1​+Z2​1​+⋯

      Total impedance:

      Ztotal=1YtotalZ_{total} = \frac{1}{Y_{total}}Ztotal​=Ytotal​1​

      Voltage:

      V(s)=Is(s)⋅ZtotalV(s) = I_s(s) \cdot Z_{total}V(s)=Is​(s)⋅Ztotal​

      Current through element i:

      Ii(s)=V(s)Zi(s)I_i(s) = \frac{V(s)}{Z_i(s)}Ii​(s)=Zi​(s)V(s)​

      Algebraic Tips

      Tip 1: Always Factor Out the Unknown

      Immediately factor out I(s) or V(s):

      Vs(s)=I(s)⋅Z1+I(s)⋅Z2=I(s)[Z1+Z2]V_s(s) = I(s) \cdot Z_1 + I(s) \cdot Z_2 = I(s)[Z_1 + Z_2]Vs​(s)=I(s)⋅Z1​+I(s)⋅Z2​=I(s)[Z1​+Z2​]

      Tip 2: Find Common Denominators Early

      100+105s=100s+105s100 + \frac{10^5}{s} = \frac{100s + 10^5}{s}100+s105​=s100s+105​

      Tip 3: Simplify Before Solving

      10100s+105=10100(s+1000)=0.1s+1000\frac{10}{100s + 10^5} = \frac{10}{100(s + 1000)} = \frac{0.1}{s + 1000}100s+10510​=100(s+1000)10​=s+10000.1​

      Tip 4: Check Units

      All impedances must be in Ohms (Ω).

      Tip 5: Verify with Limiting Cases

      • At s → 0 (DC): capacitors = open, inductors = short
      • At s → ∞ (high freq): capacitors = short, inductors = open

      What You Have Now

      After applying KVL or KCL, you have:

      1. An algebraic equation in s (not a differential equation!)
      2. Expression for V(s) or I(s) in terms of sources and impedances
      3. No initial conditions yet (added in next step if needed)

      Common Mistakes to Avoid

      Mistake 1: Forgetting to Factor

      WRONG: Leaving terms like: Vs(s)=I(s)⋅R+I(s)⋅ZLV_s(s) = I(s) \cdot R + I(s) \cdot Z_LVs​(s)=I(s)⋅R+I(s)⋅ZL​

      RIGHT: Factor immediately: Vs(s)=I(s)[R+ZL]V_s(s) = I(s)[R + Z_L]Vs​(s)=I(s)[R+ZL​]

      Mistake 2: Sign Errors

      Always use consistent sign convention:

      • Passive sign convention for impedances
      • Check source polarity carefully

      Mistake 3: Wrong Current Direction

      Current through impedance must match the voltage polarity you’re calculating.

      Mistake 4: Mixing Series and Parallel

      For series: add impedances directly For parallel: use reciprocal formula or product-over-sum

      Mistake 5: Forgetting s in Impedances

      WRONG: Z=R+L+1/CZ = R + L + 1/CZ=R+L+1/C

      RIGHT: Z=R+sL+1/(sC)Z = R + sL + 1/(sC)Z=R+sL+1/(sC)

      Next Steps

      Without initial conditions: You now have your complete equation! Skip to 06-path-a-complete-process.md for full examples.

      With initial conditions: Proceed to 03-initial-conditions.md to learn how to add them to your equation.

      Summary

      KVL/KCL in s-domain:

      1. ✓ Choose appropriate method (KVL for series, KCL for parallel)
      2. ✓ Write equation using Ohm’s law with impedances
      3. ✓ Factor out unknown (I(s) or V(s))
      4. ✓ Simplify algebraically
      5. ✓ Solve for desired variable
      6. → Result is algebraic equation in s

      Key insight: This is ALGEBRA, not differential equations! That’s the power of s-domain analysis.
