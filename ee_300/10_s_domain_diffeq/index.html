      Converting s-Domain Equations to Differential Equations

      Overview

      This document covers the reverse process: starting with an s-domain algebraic equation (from PATH A) and deriving the corresponding time-domain differential equation.

        graph LR
    A[s-Domain Algebraic Equation] --> B[Apply Inverse<br/>Correspondence]
    B --> C[Time-Domain<br/>Differential Equation]
    
    style A fill:#e8f5e9
    style C fill:#ffe0b2

      Why Do This?

      After following PATH A, you may want to:

      • Understand the physics - see what differential equation you actually solved
      • Answer exam questions - “what is the differential equation?”
      • Analyze system behavior - determine natural frequency, damping, etc.
      • Verify your work - check against PATH B result

      Inverse Correspondence Rules

      These rules convert s-domain terms back to time-domain:

      s-Domain TermTime-Domain EquivalentNotes
      V(s) or I(s)v(t) or i(t)Basic function
      sV(s)dv(t)/dtFirst derivative
      s²V(s)d²v(t)/dt²Second derivative
      s³V(s)d³v(t)/dt³Third derivative
      V(s)/s∫v(t)dtIntegration
      Constant CConstant CUnchanged
      V_s(s)v_s(t) = ℒ⁻¹{V_s(s)}Inverse transform source

      Important: Initial conditions are assumed to be handled separately (they appear as additional terms in s-domain).

      Step-by-Step Process

      Step 1: Start with s-Domain Equation

      Begin with your algebraic equation from PATH A.

      Step 2: Multiply to Clear Denominators

      Get all terms with same denominator or eliminate fractions.

      Step 3: Identify Powers of s

      Look for s⁰, s¹, s², etc. multiplying V(s) or I(s).

      Step 4: Apply Inverse Correspondence

      Replace each s-term with its time-domain equivalent.

      Step 5: Rearrange to Standard Form

      Put derivatives in descending order, right-hand side for forcing function.

      Example 1: First-Order (Series RC)

      Starting s-Domain Equation

      From PATH A Example 1:

      10s=I(s)[100+105s]\frac{10}{s} = I(s)\left[100 + \frac{10^5}{s}\right]s10​=I(s)[100+s105​]

      Or equivalently (voltage across capacitor):

      VC(s)=104s(s+1000)V_C(s) = \frac{10^4}{s(s+1000)}VC​(s)=s(s+1000)104​

      Let’s work with the impedance equation:

      Vs(s)=I(s)⋅R+I(s)⋅1sCV_s(s) = I(s) \cdot R + I(s) \cdot \frac{1}{sC}Vs​(s)=I(s)⋅R+I(s)⋅sC1​

      Step 1: Write Explicitly

      Vs(s)=R⋅I(s)+I(s)sCV_s(s) = R \cdot I(s) + \frac{I(s)}{sC}Vs​(s)=R⋅I(s)+sCI(s)​

      Step 2: Multiply by sC

      sC⋅Vs(s)=sRC⋅I(s)+I(s)sC \cdot V_s(s) = sRC \cdot I(s) + I(s)sC⋅Vs​(s)=sRC⋅I(s)+I(s)

      Step 3: Apply Inverse Correspondence

      • sC⋅Vs(s)→Cdvs(t)dtsC \cdot V_s(s) \rightarrow C\frac{dv_s(t)}{dt}sC⋅Vs​(s)→Cdtdvs​(t)​
      • sRC⋅I(s)→RCdi(t)dtsRC \cdot I(s) \rightarrow RC\frac{di(t)}{dt}sRC⋅I(s)→RCdtdi(t)​
      • I(s)→i(t)I(s) \rightarrow i(t)I(s)→i(t)

      Step 4: Time-Domain Differential Equation

      Cdvs(t)dt=RCdi(t)dt+i(t)C\frac{dv_s(t)}{dt} = RC\frac{di(t)}{dt} + i(t)Cdtdvs​(t)​=RCdtdi(t)​+i(t)

      Step 5: Alternative Form (for v_C)

      To get differential equation for v_C(t), use: i(t)=CdvC(t)dti(t) = C\frac{dv_C(t)}{dt}i(t)=CdtdvC​(t)​

      Substitute:

      Cdvs(t)dt=RC⋅Cd2vC(t)dt2+CdvC(t)dtC\frac{dv_s(t)}{dt} = RC \cdot C\frac{d^2v_C(t)}{dt^2} + C\frac{dv_C(t)}{dt}Cdtdvs​(t)​=RC⋅Cdt2d2vC​(t)​+CdtdvC​(t)​

      Simplify:

      dvs(t)dt=RCd2vC(t)dt2+dvC(t)dt\frac{dv_s(t)}{dt} = RC\frac{d^2v_C(t)}{dt^2} + \frac{dv_C(t)}{dt}dtdvs​(t)​=RCdt2d2vC​(t)​+dtdvC​(t)​

      Or integrate:

      vs(t)=RCdvC(t)dt+vC(t)v_s(t) = RC\frac{dv_C(t)}{dt} + v_C(t)vs​(t)=RCdtdvC​(t)​+vC​(t)

      Which gives:

      RCdvC(t)dt+vC(t)=vs(t)RC\frac{dv_C(t)}{dt} + v_C(t) = v_s(t)RCdtdvC​(t)​+vC​(t)=vs​(t)

      This matches PATH B Example 1!

      Example 2: First-Order (Series RL)

      Starting s-Domain Equation

      From PATH A Example 2:

      5s+2=I(s)[10+0.5s]\frac{5}{s+2} = I(s)[10 + 0.5s]s+25​=I(s)[10+0.5s]

      Step 1: Expand

      5s+2=10I(s)+0.5sI(s)\frac{5}{s+2} = 10I(s) + 0.5sI(s)s+25​=10I(s)+0.5sI(s)

      Step 2: Multiply Both Sides by 1 (already clear)

      5s+2=10I(s)+0.5sI(s)\frac{5}{s+2} = 10I(s) + 0.5sI(s)s+25​=10I(s)+0.5sI(s)

      Step 3: Apply Inverse Correspondence

      Left side: 5s+2→5e−2tu(t)\frac{5}{s+2} \rightarrow 5e^{-2t}u(t)s+25​→5e−2tu(t)

      Right side:

      • 10I(s)→10i(t)10I(s) \rightarrow 10i(t)10I(s)→10i(t)
      • 0.5sI(s)→0.5di(t)dt0.5sI(s) \rightarrow 0.5\frac{di(t)}{dt}0.5sI(s)→0.5dtdi(t)​

      Step 4: Time-Domain Differential Equation

      5e−2tu(t)=10i(t)+0.5di(t)dt5e^{-2t}u(t) = 10i(t) + 0.5\frac{di(t)}{dt}5e−2tu(t)=10i(t)+0.5dtdi(t)​

      Rearrange:

      0.5di(t)dt+10i(t)=5e−2tu(t)0.5\frac{di(t)}{dt} + 10i(t) = 5e^{-2t}u(t)0.5dtdi(t)​+10i(t)=5e−2tu(t)

      Or:

      di(t)dt+20i(t)=10e−2tu(t)\frac{di(t)}{dt} + 20i(t) = 10e^{-2t}u(t)dtdi(t)​+20i(t)=10e−2tu(t)

      This matches PATH B Example 2!

      Example 3: Second-Order (Series RLC)

      Starting s-Domain Equation

      For a series RLC circuit:

      Vs(s)=I(s)[R+sL+1sC]V_s(s) = I(s)\left[R + sL + \frac{1}{sC}\right]Vs​(s)=I(s)[R+sL+sC1​]

      Step 1: Expand

      Vs(s)=RI(s)+sLI(s)+I(s)sCV_s(s) = RI(s) + sLI(s) + \frac{I(s)}{sC}Vs​(s)=RI(s)+sLI(s)+sCI(s)​

      Step 2: Multiply by sC

      sCVs(s)=sRCI(s)+s2LCI(s)+I(s)sCV_s(s) = sRCI(s) + s^2LCI(s) + I(s)sCVs​(s)=sRCI(s)+s2LCI(s)+I(s)

      Step 3: Apply Inverse Correspondence

      • sCVs(s)→Cdvs(t)dtsCV_s(s) \rightarrow C\frac{dv_s(t)}{dt}sCVs​(s)→Cdtdvs​(t)​
      • sRCI(s)→RCdi(t)dtsRCI(s) \rightarrow RC\frac{di(t)}{dt}sRCI(s)→RCdtdi(t)​
      • s2LCI(s)→LCd2i(t)dt2s^2LCI(s) \rightarrow LC\frac{d^2i(t)}{dt^2}s2LCI(s)→LCdt2d2i(t)​
      • I(s)→i(t)I(s) \rightarrow i(t)I(s)→i(t)

      Step 4: Time-Domain Differential Equation

      Cdvs(t)dt=RCdi(t)dt+LCd2i(t)dt2+i(t)C\frac{dv_s(t)}{dt} = RC\frac{di(t)}{dt} + LC\frac{d^2i(t)}{dt^2} + i(t)Cdtdvs​(t)​=RCdtdi(t)​+LCdt2d2i(t)​+i(t)

      Rearrange:

      LCd2i(t)dt2+RCdi(t)dt+i(t)=Cdvs(t)dtLC\frac{d^2i(t)}{dt^2} + RC\frac{di(t)}{dt} + i(t) = C\frac{dv_s(t)}{dt}LCdt2d2i(t)​+RCdtdi(t)​+i(t)=Cdtdvs​(t)​

      This is a second-order differential equation.

      For a step input v_s(t) = V₀u(t), the derivative is V₀δ(t):

      LCd2i(t)dt2+RCdi(t)dt+i(t)=CV0δ(t)LC\frac{d^2i(t)}{dt^2} + RC\frac{di(t)}{dt} + i(t) = CV_0\delta(t)LCdt2d2i(t)​+RCdtdi(t)​+i(t)=CV0​δ(t)

      Example 4: Parallel RLC Circuit

      Starting s-Domain Equation

      Is(s)=V(s)[1R+1sL+sC]I_s(s) = V(s)\left[\frac{1}{R} + \frac{1}{sL} + sC\right]Is​(s)=V(s)[R1​+sL1​+sC]

      Step 1: Expand

      Is(s)=V(s)R+V(s)sL+sCV(s)I_s(s) = \frac{V(s)}{R} + \frac{V(s)}{sL} + sCV(s)Is​(s)=RV(s)​+sLV(s)​+sCV(s)

      Step 2: Multiply by sL

      sLIs(s)=sLV(s)R+V(s)+s2LCV(s)sLI_s(s) = \frac{sLV(s)}{R} + V(s) + s^2LCV(s)sLIs​(s)=RsLV(s)​+V(s)+s2LCV(s)

      Step 3: Apply Inverse Correspondence

      • sLIs(s)→Ldis(t)dtsLI_s(s) \rightarrow L\frac{di_s(t)}{dt}sLIs​(s)→Ldtdis​(t)​
      • sLV(s)R→LRdv(t)dt\frac{sLV(s)}{R} \rightarrow \frac{L}{R}\frac{dv(t)}{dt}RsLV(s)​→RL​dtdv(t)​
      • V(s)→v(t)V(s) \rightarrow v(t)V(s)→v(t)
      • s2LCV(s)→LCd2v(t)dt2s^2LCV(s) \rightarrow LC\frac{d^2v(t)}{dt^2}s2LCV(s)→LCdt2d2v(t)​

      Step 4: Time-Domain Differential Equation

      Ldis(t)dt=LRdv(t)dt+v(t)+LCd2v(t)dt2L\frac{di_s(t)}{dt} = \frac{L}{R}\frac{dv(t)}{dt} + v(t) + LC\frac{d^2v(t)}{dt^2}Ldtdis​(t)​=RL​dtdv(t)​+v(t)+LCdt2d2v(t)​

      Rearrange:

      LCd2v(t)dt2+LRdv(t)dt+v(t)=Ldis(t)dtLC\frac{d^2v(t)}{dt^2} + \frac{L}{R}\frac{dv(t)}{dt} + v(t) = L\frac{di_s(t)}{dt}LCdt2d2v(t)​+RL​dtdv(t)​+v(t)=Ldtdis​(t)​

      For step input i_s(t) = I₀u(t):

      LCd2v(t)dt2+LRdv(t)dt+v(t)=LI0δ(t)LC\frac{d^2v(t)}{dt^2} + \frac{L}{R}\frac{dv(t)}{dt} + v(t) = LI_0\delta(t)LCdt2d2v(t)​+RL​dtdv(t)​+v(t)=LI0​δ(t)

      Extracting System Parameters

      Once you have the differential equation in standard form, you can identify system characteristics.

      First-Order Standard Form

      τdy(t)dt+y(t)=Ku(t)\tau\frac{dy(t)}{dt} + y(t) = Ku(t)τdtdy(t)​+y(t)=Ku(t)

      Where:

      • τ = time constant (seconds)
      • K = DC gain

      For RC circuit: τ = RC For RL circuit: τ = L/R

      Second-Order Standard Form

      d2y(t)dt2+2ζωndy(t)dt+ωn2y(t)=Ku(t)\frac{d^2y(t)}{dt^2} + 2\zeta\omega_n\frac{dy(t)}{dt} + \omega_n^2y(t) = Ku(t)dt2d2y(t)​+2ζωn​dtdy(t)​+ωn2​y(t)=Ku(t)

      Where:

      • ω_n = natural frequency (rad/s)
      • ζ = damping ratio (unitless)
      • K = DC gain

      Example: Series RLC

      From: LCd2idt2+RCdidt+i=CdvsdtLC\frac{d^2i}{dt^2} + RC\frac{di}{dt} + i = C\frac{dv_s}{dt}LCdt2d2i​+RCdtdi​+i=Cdtdvs​​

      Divide by LC:

      d2idt2+RLdidt+1LCi=1Ldvsdt\frac{d^2i}{dt^2} + \frac{R}{L}\frac{di}{dt} + \frac{1}{LC}i = \frac{1}{L}\frac{dv_s}{dt}dt2d2i​+LR​dtdi​+LC1​i=L1​dtdvs​​

      Identify:

      • ωn2=1LC\omega_n^2 = \frac{1}{LC}ωn2​=LC1​ → ωn=1LC\omega_n = \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}}ωn​=LC​1​
      • 2ζωn=RL2\zeta\omega_n = \frac{R}{L}2ζωn​=LR​ → ζ=R2CL\zeta = \frac{R}{2}\sqrt{\frac{C}{L}}ζ=2R​LC​​

      Damping Categories

      • Overdamped: ζ > 1 (two real poles)
      • Critically damped: ζ = 1 (repeated real pole)
      • Underdamped: ζ < 1 (complex conjugate poles)

      Converting Back to s-Domain (Verification)

      You can verify your differential equation by applying Laplace transform:

      Time Domain:

      d2i(t)dt2+2ζωndi(t)dt+ωn2i(t)=u(t)\frac{d^2i(t)}{dt^2} + 2\zeta\omega_n\frac{di(t)}{dt} + \omega_n^2i(t) = u(t)dt2d2i(t)​+2ζωn​dtdi(t)​+ωn2​i(t)=u(t)

      Apply Laplace:

      s2I(s)−si(0)−i′(0)+2ζωn[sI(s)−i(0)]+ωn2I(s)=1ss^2I(s) - si(0) - i'(0) + 2\zeta\omega_n[sI(s) - i(0)] + \omega_n^2I(s) = \frac{1}{s}s2I(s)−si(0)−i′(0)+2ζωn​[sI(s)−i(0)]+ωn2​I(s)=s1​

      With zero initial conditions:

      I(s)[s2+2ζωns+ωn2]=1sI(s)[s^2 + 2\zeta\omega_n s + \omega_n^2] = \frac{1}{s}I(s)[s2+2ζωn​s+ωn2​]=s1​I(s)=1s(s2+2ζωns+ωn2)I(s) = \frac{1}{s(s^2 + 2\zeta\omega_n s + \omega_n^2)}I(s)=s(s2+2ζωn​s+ωn2​)1​

      This should match your PATH A result!

      Common Pitfalls

      Pitfall 1: Forgetting to Clear Denominators

      Before applying inverse correspondence, ensure all terms are polynomials in s, not fractions.

      Pitfall 2: Wrong Correspondence

      WRONG: V(s)s→v(t)t\frac{V(s)}{s} \rightarrow \frac{v(t)}{t}sV(s)​→tv(t)​

      RIGHT: V(s)s→∫v(t)dt\frac{V(s)}{s} \rightarrow \int v(t)dtsV(s)​→∫v(t)dt

      Pitfall 3: Mixing Variables

      Stay consistent - if solving for i(t), don’t introduce v(t) without substituting relationships.

      Pitfall 4: Source Function Confusion

      The inverse transform of V_s(s) gives v_s(t), but you may need its derivative for the RHS.
