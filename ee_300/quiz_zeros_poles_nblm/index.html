      I. Comprehensive Theory Overview: ZSR, ZIR, and Network Functions

      The total response of a linear circuit can be separated using the principle of superposition into two components: the Zero-Input Response (ZIR) and the Zero-State Response (ZSR).

      A. Zero-Input Response (ZIR)

      The Zero-Input Response (ZIR) is the circuit behavior due entirely to stored energy (initial conditions) in energy storage elements (capacitors and inductors).

      • Definition: ZIR is the response assuming there is zero external input or source.
      • Origin: It is derived from the initial voltage across a capacitor, Vc(0)V_c(0)Vc​(0), or the initial current through an inductor, IL(0)I_L(0)IL​(0).
      • Time Domain Connection: The ZIR is solely composed of the natural response of the circuit.

      B. Zero-State Response (ZSR)

      The Zero-State Response (ZSR) is the circuit behavior due entirely to the external input sources.

      • Definition: ZSR is the response when the initial state of the circuit (stored energy) is set to zero.
      • Input Requirement: The ZSR occurs for any input, provided that before time t<0t<0t<0, no energy is stored in the network.

      C. Network Functions (H(s)H(s)H(s))

      The Network Function, H(s)H(s)H(s) (or T(s)T(s)T(s)), is the mathematical representation of the circuit’s dynamic behavior in the sss-domain and is fundamentally related to the ZSR.

      • Definition: The network function is the ratio of the ZSR output in the sss-domain to the ZSR input in the sss-domain. H(s)=Output Variable(s)Input Variable(s)H(s) = \frac{\text{Output Variable}(s)}{\text{Input Variable}(s)}H(s)=Input Variable(s)Output Variable(s)​
      • Requirement: Network functions are found by explicitly setting all initial condition sources to zero.
      • Time Domain Connection: If the input is Vin(s)V_{in}(s)Vin​(s), the output ZSR is calculated as Vout(s)=H(s)⋅Vin(s)V_{out}(s) = H(s) \cdot V_{in}(s)Vout​(s)=H(s)⋅Vin​(s).
      • Types:
        • Transfer Function (H(s)H(s)H(s) or T(s)T(s)T(s)): Input and output are at two different terminal pairs.
        • Driving-Point Impedance (Z(s)Z(s)Z(s)): Ratio of voltage to current (V/IV/IV/I) measured at the same terminal pair.
        • Driving-Point Admittance (Y(s)Y(s)Y(s)): Ratio of current to voltage (I/VI/VI/V) measured at the same terminal pair; Y(s)=1/Z(s)Y(s) = 1/Z(s)Y(s)=1/Z(s).

      B. What are Zeros?

      1. Definition: The zeros of the circuit are the roots of the numerator of the network function, N(s)=0N(s)=0N(s)=0.

        • Differential Equation: Zeros are determined by the input side (right side) of the differential equation.

      4. Relationship Between H(s)H(s)H(s) and Differential Equations (Diffeq)

      The structure of the network function directly dictates the form of the input-output differential equation (ODE) of the circuit.

      Poles (Denominator D(s)D(s)D(s)) →\rightarrow→ Output (ZIR/Natural Response)

      • Response Link: Poles determine the exponents (rates) of the terms that form the natural response (and thus the ZIR). Poles are the rates of exponentials that are “always” in the output.

      Zeros (Numerator N(s)N(s)N(s)) →\rightarrow→ Input (ZSR/Forced Response Interaction)

      • Diffeq Side: The coefficients of the numerator polynomial N(s)N(s)N(s) correspond to the coefficients of the input variable and its derivatives on the right side of the ODE.
      • Response Link: Zeros determine the frequencies (rates) of exponentials that are never in the output. If an exponential input occurs at the frequency of a circuit zero, that signal is blocked or vanishes in the output (provided the input pole is not of a higher order than the circuit zero).
